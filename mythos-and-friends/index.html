1. About this article

Anthropic's announcement of Claude Mythos Preview in April 2026, and the Project Glasswing programme that accompanied it, made one thing concrete that the security community had been discussing in abstract for years: frontier AI models can now find and weaponise software vulnerabilities at machine speed, with minimal human guidance. Independent evaluation by the UK AI Security Institute and others has corroborated the headline claims. Whether or not Mythos itself ever escapes its controlled-access regime, equivalent capabilities are arriving in other models, including ones with far weaker safeguards.

Banks and other financial institutions are exposed in particular ways. They run consequential, real-time, interconnected systems, sit on regulated data and on the means of payment, and face well-funded and unusually patient adversaries. Most importantly, the historical defensive playbook - periodic scans, signature-based detection, perimeter trust, eventually-consistent patch SLAs - was built around threat actors operating at human pace. Speed has changed; the rest of the playbook has not yet caught up.

This article is my attempt at writing down what to actually do about it, in concrete terms. It is meant for cybersecurity and IT engineers: CISOs and their deputies, SOC leads, vulnerability management, IAM, security architecture, detection engineers, incident response, network and endpoint teams. It is not a strategy paper, a regulatory primer, or a vendor comparison. Where it makes sense to be opinionated, I have been - and the underlying view, which I will return to at the end, is that the only realistic answer to AI-augmented attackers is to master the use of AI on the defensive side at least as competently.

How the playbook is structured

Each measure is presented as an action card with a fixed structure:

  • ID - short reference (S-xx for short term, M-xx for medium term, L-xx for long term).

  • Title and complexity rating with indicative effort.

  • Risk covered - the specific risk the measure mitigates.

  • Attack vector - MITRE ATT&CK tactics and the concrete adversary actions the measure obstructs.

  • Prerequisites - what must already be in place, including dependencies on other measures.

  • Description and, where useful, a Technical detail block with example configurations, detection logic, query patterns, or architectural notes.

Complexity scale

Complexity reflects engineering and operational difficulty, not strategic importance:

  • Trivial - configuration change, hours of work, no architectural impact.

  • Low - tooling-supported, days of work, single team.

  • Medium - sprint-scale, weeks, two or three teams in coordination.

  • High - programme-scale, months, multi-team, change management non-trivial.

  • Very High - architectural, multi-quarter, requires new capability or significant reskilling.

A word on what this is not

Most of the measures listed are not new categories of control. They are tunings, accelerations, or extensions of work that mature cyber organisations already do. None of them should be read as a recommendation to procure a new platform unless the technical detail block explicitly says so. The premise throughout is that the capability you need is usually already deployed somewhere in the estate, and the gap is most often in coverage, tuning, integration, or speed.

The article also does not pretend to cover every relevant control. Cryptography lifecycle management, data classification, application-security testing in the development pipeline, fraud detection, OT security in adjacent business units - all matter, all interact with the AI threat picture, and none are covered here. I have deliberately scoped this to the controls most directly affected by the frontier-AI capability shift.

2. Operating assumptions and threat model

The measures in this article are written for an adversary that has access - directly, through online services that fail to refuse offensive requests, or through open-weights models - to capabilities of the same class as Anthropic's Claude Mythos Preview. These capabilities are not theoretical. They have been independently corroborated by the UK AI Security Institute and academic groups. The five points below are what I take from that, and what the rest of the article is built on.

2.1 Compressed disclose-to-exploit window

Weaponised exploits for newly disclosed CVEs in widely deployed software now appear within hours of disclosure, not days. This is consistent with the latest ENISA threat-landscape reporting and with observed industry behaviour. Patch SLAs that assume a several-day exploitation lag are obsolete for internet-facing assets, and probably for many internal ones too.

2.2 Autonomous, multi-step adversary operations

An adversary can now run continuous, agentic offensive operations against a target environment for extended periods at low cost, parallelising across many targets. AISI's evaluation showed Mythos completing the full 32-step "The Last Ones" simulated network attack end-to-end. Detection logic that triggers on individual TTPs in isolation will be evaded. What you actually need is detection that fires on the kill chain as a whole.

2.3 Industrialised social engineering

Any individual employee with a public footprint can be the target of bespoke, multi-channel, multi-language social engineering - including voice and video deepfakes referencing real context - at a marginal cost per target that is now near zero. Phishing-resilience programmes built to defend against generic-quality lures are no longer fit for purpose.

2.4 Vendor and open-source dependency exposure

An adversary with frontier-model assistance can find vulnerabilities in almost any non-trivial codebase faster than the code's maintainer can patch them. For most institutions, this exposure is dominated by third-party software, where direct technical mitigation is limited and the only realistic response is structural - blast-radius reduction, SBOM-driven detection, vendor assurance.

2.5 Detection telemetry as adversary target

A sophisticated adversary will try to disable, blind, or evade telemetry collection early in an operation. EDR tampering, log forwarder kill, and timestomp are now baseline expected behaviours. Detection logic must include detection of detection-disablement, otherwise the whole investment becomes worthless the moment the agent goes silent.

2.6 What is not assumed

  • That the adversary has direct access to Mythos Preview itself. Glasswing access is tightly controlled. The threat is from capability proliferation, not from Mythos as a specific weapon.

  • That no human is in the loop on the offensive side. Frontier models accelerate human attackers, they do not yet replace them. This affects pace and scale, not goals or targets.

  • That existing controls are worthless. They are not. The point of the article is to identify where existing controls need recalibration, extension, or coverage closure.

  • That the article addresses everything the AI threat changes. It does not. Two adjacent vectors are deliberately out of scope and worth a reader's attention elsewhere: synthetic identity at account opening (AI-generated documents and deepfake video KYC), and customer-channel abuse (the bank's own chatbot and IVR as a recon and social-engineering surface, including voice-authentication bypass for telephone banking). These belong in fraud, KYC, and contact-centre programmes; mentioning them here so that nothing assumes they are covered by anything that follows.

2.7 The asymmetry, and what it implies for ambition

There is one consequence of all of the above that I think organisations need to internalise more bluntly than they tend to. The attacker only needs one serious vulnerability to do the job. The defender has to be right about every one of them. That asymmetry was already uncomfortable when humans were doing the looking; it becomes far worse when AI can check them all, automatically, in parallel, around the clock. My belief is that internal cyber metrics should target 100% compliance as their stated ambition - not because anyone realistically expects to hit it, but because anything less builds in tolerance for exactly the kind of single overlooked weakness that a frontier-model-assisted attacker will find first. The targets and the SLAs in the rest of this article are written with that posture in mind. None of this conflicts with the realistic statements elsewhere - notably the detection-coverage curve in L-03 - because the ambition is about the goal you set, not the number you forecast hitting next quarter.

2.8 Developer tooling has become its own supply-chain surface

Section 2.4 covered third-party software dependencies as a vector. There is a parallel vector that has emerged sharply over the last 18 months and that the traditional supply-chain framing does not capture: the developer's own workstation and IDE. AI coding assistants (GitHub Copilot, Cursor, Claude Code, JetBrains AI, and equivalents) routinely send code context to external APIs as part of normal operation. MCP servers, IDE plugins, and VS Code or JetBrains extensions execute with full IDE process permissions and have direct access to source code, environment variables, and any credentials present on the developer's machine. Several material incidents in 2025 and 2026 have come from poisoned or malicious MCP servers and from compromised marketplace extensions with seven-figure install counts.

For an internally-developed application, this is now the path of least resistance from internet to codebase to environment secrets, with no network exploit required. It is upstream of M-02 (SBOM) and M-09 (third-party software): those measures inspect the output of the development process, but they do not see the vector if the development environment itself is compromised.

A full treatment of this is its own piece of work, and I am not going to pretend it fits in this article. As a starting set of minimum controls that any institution should already have in flight while the larger programme is scoped:

  • An approved-tools register for AI coding assistants and MCP servers. Anything not on the register is denied at the egress proxy and at the IDE plugin manifest level.

  • Plugin and extension governance for VS Code, JetBrains, and equivalents. Block the marketplace's general install path; install from a curated internal mirror only. The extensions that get into the mirror have been reviewed.

  • Network isolation for build agents and CI runners. Developer-workstation network rules are not the same as build-pipeline network rules; in particular, build agents should not have outbound access to public AI inference endpoints unless a specific pipeline step requires it.

  • Secret scanning triggered on AI tool activity. If an AI coding assistant has just transmitted code from a repository, scan the next commit and the recent diff for credentials with a tighter threshold than baseline.

  • Hardware-bound credentials on developer workstations for anything that touches production. A compromised IDE process should not be able to use those credentials away from the machine they are bound to.

None of these is a complete answer; they are the controls that close the most obvious holes while a proper programme is designed. I am flagging the vector here so it is not assumed away by anything that follows.

3. Cross-cutting principles

Seven principles run through the measures that follow. They are stated here once so the action cards do not need to repeat them.

3.1 Speed-to-decision over information completeness

Detection and response pipelines have to be set up for fast, defensible decisions on incomplete information. A 30-minute manual triage that ends in certainty is worse than a 2-minute decision that errs on the side of containment, given the pace the adversary is now able to operate at. This principle sits behind several of the short-term measures on SOC automation and emergency change.

3.2 Containment by default

Architecture and IAM choices have to start from the assumption that any single asset, account, or service is compromised. The cost of lateral movement that you can impose on the adversary is the single most useful thing you can invest in. Microsegmentation, just-in-time elevation, and short-lived credentials all follow from this.

3.3 Telemetry first

Controls without telemetry are theatrical. Every measure proposed below produces or assumes telemetry. Detection of control failure is itself a control. Saying "we have an EDR" is meaningless without coverage metrics, tamper detection, and a response that has actually been exercised.

3.4 Detect on intent, not artefact

AI-augmented adversaries can mass-produce previously-unseen artefacts (binaries, payloads, infrastructure). Detection logic built on hashes, lists, and signatures will degrade quickly. Detection that fires on observable intent - "a workstation account is performing reconnaissance against the directory", "a service account is reading unusual paths" - degrades far more slowly.

3.5 Identity is the perimeter

In a hybrid-cloud, SaaS-heavy estate, the network is no longer a meaningful trust boundary. Identity, device posture, and session context are. Controls that act on identity scale across the estate. Controls that depend on network position do not.

3.6 Build for the next CVE, not the last incident

Every measure here should be judged on whether it would have helped against the next, unknown vulnerability or campaign, not on whether it would have caught the most recent one. AI-augmented attackers close the gap between disclosure and exploitation; the only durable answer is generic hardening, not signature chasing.

3.7 The quality of the underlying reference data is non-negotiable

In my own practice, I keep insisting on this point: no cyber protection process can be efficient if the reference data underneath it is wrong, stale, or partial. Asset inventories, configuration baselines, ownership registers, network maps, identity catalogues - the ground truth that every other control depends on. Microsegmentation policies authored against an incomplete dependency map will break applications. Vulnerability prioritisation against a CMDB that does not flag internet exposure will mis-prioritise. Detection rules tuned against an estate the SOC does not fully know will leave blind spots. Spending on shiny new platforms while the basic registers are unreliable is one of the most expensive mistakes you can make. Everything in this article assumes that the reference data work is taken seriously.

4. Short term (0-6 months)

Twelve measures, ordered roughly by sequencing dependency. Most are tuning and coverage-closure work on existing capabilities, plus a small number that require lightweight tooling additions. None require new architecture or major procurement.

S-01 Recalibrate vulnerability SLAs against EPSS, KEV, and exposure

Low

2-4 weeks
Risk covered Compromise via newly-disclosed CVE before existing SLA window expires.
Attack vector ATT&CK TA0001 Initial Access via T1190 Exploit Public-Facing Application, with follow-on T1212 Exploitation for Credential Access (TA0006) and T1068 Exploitation for Privilege Escalation (TA0004) once the foothold is established.
Prerequisites Vulnerability scanner producing CVE-tagged findings (Tenable / Qualys / Rapid7 / similar). Asset inventory with internet-exposure flag. CMDB linkage for asset criticality. EPSS and KEV feeds (free).

Replace flat CVSS-driven SLAs with a multi-signal prioritisation model. CVSS measures severity if exploited; EPSS estimates exploitation probability in the next 30 days; KEV confirms current in-the-wild exploitation. Combined with asset exposure and criticality, this allows defensible patch ordering under volume pressure.

The action is policy and tooling configuration, not new capability. EPSS data is published daily by FIRST.org as JSON/CSV; most modern scanners now ingest it natively. KEV is published by CISA and is freely consumed.

Technical detail

Suggested tier definitions for internet-exposed assets:

  • P0 - On KEV, OR EPSS ≥ 0.6, OR vendor advisory references active exploitation. SLA: emergency change, target patch within 8 hours, compensating control within 4.

  • P1 - CVSS ≥ 8.0 AND EPSS ≥ 0.1, not yet KEV. SLA: 24 hours.

  • P2 - CVSS ≥ 7.0 OR EPSS ≥ 0.05. SLA: 7 days.

  • P3 - Below thresholds. SLA: standard 30-day cycle.

For non-internet-exposed assets, shift each tier by one level. Document the SLA in policy; expose it as a metric in the patch dashboard; review monthly with risk-management oversight.

EPSS feed integration is a one-time job:

# Daily EPSS ingestion (cron @ 06:00 UTC, after FIRST publish)

curl -s https://epss.empiricalsecurity.com/epss_scores-current.csv.gz | \

gunzip | \

awk -F, 'NR>2 && $2>=0.05 {print $1","$2","$3}' > /var/data/epss-high.csv

# Join with scanner output keyed on CVE-ID; do not overwrite history.

# Persist daily snapshots - EPSS trend (doubling in a week) matters more than absolute value.

S-02 Establish an emergency-change pathway for security-driven changes

Low

2-3 weeks
Risk covered The existing change-management cycle becomes the bottleneck on patch speed, leaving the system exposed for longer than the adversary needs to weaponise the vulnerability.
Attack vector Same as S-01. The emergency pathway is the operational counterpart to S-01's revised SLAs: a policy SLA without a delivery channel is meaningless.
Prerequisites Existing change-management process. Engineering rota. Roll-back plans for in-scope systems. Pre-defined reviewer set with delegated authority to approve emergency changes.

Create a pre-authorised, well-instrumented emergency change pathway specifically for security patches and compensating controls. The pathway must be invokable by the SOC duty officer or vulnerability-management lead without case-by-case CAB approval, with mandatory after-the-fact review.

Every emergency change must produce a structured record (asset, CVE, decision criteria, change applied, rollback verified, after-action review). Without this, the pathway will either be rejected by governance functions or quietly bypassed under pressure.

Technical detail

Minimum control set for the pathway to be defensible:

  • Two-person authorisation captured in the change-management tool, with one being from a defined cyber role.

  • Automatic notification to system owner and CISO duty officer.

  • Mandatory rollback test or pre-staged rollback plan.

  • Twenty-four-hour after-action review with documented disposition (accept / make permanent / unwind).

  • Quarterly metrics review by an independent function: rate of pathway use, false-emergency rate, post-incident review of all uses.

S-03 Internet-facing attack surface inventory and reduction

Low

4-6 weeks
Risk covered Unknown or undocumented internet-exposed assets become the path of least resistance for autonomous reconnaissance.
Attack vector ATT&CK TA0043 Reconnaissance via T1595 Active Scanning, T1592 Gather Victim Host Information; TA0001 Initial Access via T1190.
Prerequisites External attack-surface management capability (commercial: Censys, Shodan-based ASM, Microsoft Defender EASM, Mandiant ASM; open-source feasible for smaller perimeters). Authoritative DNS records. Public IP allocation registry.

Run a fresh external attack-surface discovery against all known IP ranges, registered domains, and cloud-tenant boundaries. Reconcile against the asset inventory. Triage every unmatched finding into one of three buckets: (1) legitimate but unrecorded, (2) abandoned and removable, (3) shadow IT.

The objective is not perfect inventory completeness - that is a longer programme - but to get rid of the easiest, lowest-effort adversary targets: forgotten dev environments, abandoned subdomains, exposed admin interfaces, expired certificates on still-resolving names.

Technical detail

High-value findings to look for explicitly:

  • Authentication portals (RDP, SSH, VPN admin, Citrix, vCenter) reachable from the public internet without IP allow-listing.

  • Cloud storage buckets with public read/write.

  • Subdomain takeover candidates (CNAME pointing at decommissioned cloud resources).

  • Exposed management planes for SaaS connectors (CI/CD runners, observability platforms).

  • Forgotten WAF-bypass routes (hostnames resolving directly to origin IPs).

Treat every unmatched finding as P1 by default. Force a within-72h decision on remove vs. record vs. accept.

S-04 Phishing-simulation programme uplift for AI-grade lures

Low

6-8 weeks
Risk covered Personnel compromise via high-fidelity, multi-channel social engineering (text + voice + video), particularly against high-value populations (executives, treasury, payments authorisers, M&A teams).
Attack vector ATT&CK TA0001 Initial Access via T1566 Phishing (sub-techniques .001 attachment, .002 link, .003 service); T1684.001 Social Engineering: Impersonation (formerly T1656, revoked in ATT&CK v19); T1598 Phishing for Information.
Prerequisites Existing phishing-simulation platform (KnowBe4, Cofense, Hoxhunt, etc.). HR list of high-value populations with regular updates. Approval for use of generative tools for lure creation. Communications plan for non-test scenarios.

Refresh the simulation programme to include language-localised, contextually personalised lures generated with current-generation tools. Move from annual to quarterly cadence for high-value populations; include voice (synthesised audio messages) and where feasible video deepfake exposures.

Two things this is meant to achieve. First, re-baseline what counts as a tolerable click-through rate: that number will rise materially once the lure quality matches what real attackers can now produce. Second, train the high-value populations specifically on out-of-band verification protocols, so that any irregular payment, credential, or access request gets verified through a separate channel regardless of how convincing the original looks.

Technical detail

Programme content elements:

  • Multi-language lures matching the languages of the target population (not English only).

  • Personalisation drawing on legitimate public context (recent corporate announcements, public LinkedIn activity, conference attendance) to match real attacker tradecraft.

  • Voice-call follow-up referencing a real meeting, for the executive simulation cohort.

  • A small number of "impossible" lures (deepfake video of a known person making an irregular request) per cycle, for the executive cohort only, with mandatory debrief.

Out-of-band verification protocol: any request for payment authorisation, credential disclosure, urgent access provisioning, or signing of unfamiliar documents must be verified through a pre-agreed second channel. Document the protocol; train against it; test compliance via simulation.

S-05 Privileged-access tightening: just-in-time elevation and session recording

Medium

3-4 months
Risk covered Standing administrative privilege gives the adversary a small set of very high-value credentials to steal. Session recording is the audit trail that survives EDR tampering.
Attack vector ATT&CK TA0004 Privilege Escalation via T1078 Valid Accounts (.002 Domain, .004 Cloud); TA0008 Lateral Movement via T1021 Remote Services; TA0006 Credential Access broadly.
Prerequisites Existing PAM platform (CyberArk, Delinea, BeyondTrust, HashiCorp Vault, or cloud-native equivalents). Authoritative inventory of privileged accounts (human + service). Tier-0 / Tier-1 / Tier-2 administrative model defined.

Reduce standing administrative privilege to the minimum compatible with operations. Implement just-in-time elevation with explicit time-bounded grants for all interactive admin sessions. Enable session recording for Tier-0 and Tier-1 administrative activity, with recordings written to write-once storage outside the administered domain.

This is mostly about getting more value out of the existing PAM deployment, not procuring a new platform. The most common dysfunction I see in mature PAM environments is that elevation is technically gated but operationally easy: admins request and are auto-approved within seconds, which defeats the point. The fix is to make approval friction proportional to scope (Tier-0 requires a named human approver, Tier-1 a queue-based on-call gate) and to enforce aggressive session timeouts.

Technical detail

Tier model (illustrative):

  • Tier-0 - directory services, identity providers, certificate authorities, key management. Standing privilege: zero. Approval: named human in another team. Session: recorded, MFA at elevation and at action, max 4 hours.

  • Tier-1 - production application administrators, database admins, hypervisor admins. Standing privilege: zero. Approval: queue-based with on-call gate. Max session: 8 hours.

  • Tier-2 - workstation admin, helpdesk. Standing privilege: limited per-task. Just-in-time for everything else.

Service accounts deserve separate treatment - see M-04.

S-06 MFA hardening: phishing-resistant factors for high-value workflows

Medium

3-6 months
Risk covered Push-bombing, MFA fatigue, and adversary-in-the-middle proxy attacks (Evilginx-class) defeat first-generation MFA, particularly against AI-augmented social engineering.
Attack vector ATT&CK T1621 Multi-Factor Authentication Request Generation; T1556.006 Modify Authentication Process: MFA; T1111 Multi-Factor Authentication Interception.
Prerequisites Existing MFA deployment. IdP supporting WebAuthn/FIDO2 (Entra ID, Okta, Ping/ForgeRock, all support it). Hardware key procurement budget for high-value populations or platform-bound passkeys (Windows Hello for Business, macOS platform passkeys).

Migrate from push-notification or TOTP MFA to phishing-resistant factors (WebAuthn/FIDO2, platform passkeys, or hardware tokens such as YubiKey 5 series) for all high-value populations and all administrative authentication. Push and TOTP can remain for low-risk consumer flows but should be removed from any path that grants administrative or financially material access.

Phishing-resistant MFA defeats the entire class of AiTM proxy attacks because the WebAuthn challenge is cryptographically bound to the origin domain. An attacker proxying login.example.com through their own evil.com cannot complete the WebAuthn ceremony because the browser will refuse.

Technical detail

Migration sequence (avoid the all-at-once trap):

  • Phase 1: enable WebAuthn alongside existing factors for all users. No removal yet.

  • Phase 2: enforce WebAuthn-only for administrative authentication and Tier-0/Tier-1 access. Block other factors at conditional-access policy level.

  • Phase 3: enforce WebAuthn-only for high-value workflows (payment authorisation, treasury, M&A document access).

  • Phase 4: extend to general user population, with break-glass alternative for genuine hardware-loss cases.

Disable SMS-based MFA wherever it can be replaced by a phishing-resistant factor, and certainly for any administrative or privileged account. SMS still satisfies some regulatory baselines, but against modern AiTM tooling it does not slow a determined adversary down meaningfully - treat it as a coverage floor, not as protection.

Conditional access example (Entra ID, in pseudo-config):

policy: "admin-mfa-fido2-only"

conditions:

users: group("PrivilegedRoleAssignees")

applications: any

controls:

grant:

authentication_strength: "phishing_resistant_mfa"

block_legacy_auth: true

sign_in_risk: any

S-07 EDR coverage and tamper-detection audit

Low

3-4 weeks
Risk covered Endpoint compromise on assets where EDR is absent, mis-deployed, or has been silenced by the adversary as the first action of an operation.
Attack vector ATT&CK TA0005 Defense Evasion via T1562.001 Disable or Modify Tools; T1070 Indicator Removal; T1218 System Binary Proxy Execution.
Prerequisites Existing EDR platform (CrowdStrike, SentinelOne, Microsoft Defender for Endpoint, or similar). Authoritative endpoint inventory.

Reconcile the EDR deployment census against the endpoint inventory. Investigate every gap. Implement tamper-detection alerting that fires when an EDR agent stops reporting, is uninstalled, or has its kernel components modified, with the alert routed to the SOC at the same priority as a confirmed malicious-binary detection.

Tamper detection is non-negotiable in the post-Mythos threat model. Sophisticated adversaries disable EDR as part of the tooling-disable stage of an operation; if that disablement does not generate an alert, the entire EDR investment is wasted from that point forward.

Technical detail

Specific signals to alert on (vendor-agnostic):

  • Agent service stopped, paused, or uninstalled - telemetry stream interruption longer than a tunable threshold (5-15 minutes for endpoints, 1 minute for servers).

  • Kernel driver or system extension unloading on the protected endpoint.

  • Local administrator group modifications outside change windows.

  • Group Policy or MDM policy changes that affect EDR posture.

  • Suspicious use of vendor-supplied uninstaller tools (RMM platforms, MDM unenrolment tools, vendor maintenance utilities).

Alerts on tamper events must NOT be suppressible by the local administrator or by the host-agent itself. Telemetry bypass paths (write-only forwarders, separate channel) are valuable for the highest-tier hosts.

S-08 Identity-protection alerts: impossible travel, token theft, and session anomalies

Low

2-4 weeks
Risk covered Token theft (cookie or refresh-token exfiltration) bypasses MFA entirely once the session is established; impossible-travel events identify credential reuse from compromised endpoints.
Attack vector ATT&CK T1539 Steal Web Session Cookie; T1528 Steal Application Access Token; T1550.004 Web Session Cookie.
Prerequisites IdP with risk-based authentication (Entra ID Identity Protection, Okta ThreatInsight, PingOne Protect, Ping/ForgeRock Autonomous Access). SIEM with IdP log ingestion. Geo-IP enrichment.

Most of these signals are already available in the IdP - most banks I've seen have them licensed and unconfigured. The job is to turn them on, tune the thresholds, and wire the resulting signals into SOC queues with a defined response playbook.

Token theft is one of the most under-detected real-world attack patterns in 2026. WebAuthn (S-06) hardens the front door, but it does not stop a stolen session token being replayed from attacker infrastructure. The signal that catches that is location, device, or user-agent inconsistency on token use after the session has been established.

Technical detail

Detection logic to enable (vendor-mapped names vary):

  • Impossible travel - same user, two authentications from geographically inconsistent locations within plausible-flight-time gap.

  • Anonymous IP - sign-in or token use from Tor, known VPN exit, or hosting-provider IP not previously associated with the user.

  • Unusual sign-in properties - new device, new browser, new ASN combination on a high-privilege account.

  • Token issued from one IP, used from another (in particular, issued from corporate VPN, used from cloud-hosting IP within minutes - characteristic of token theft via infostealer).

  • Service-principal anomalies - OAuth consent grants by users to newly-registered apps; high-privilege scope grants; credential additions to existing apps.

Expect the initial deployment to be noisy. The tuning is the work; budget for it.

S-09 Egress filtering and DNS sinkholing for known-bad infrastructure

Low

4-6 weeks
Risk covered Command-and-control beaconing, exfiltration, and second-stage payload retrieval over outbound connections from compromised internal hosts.
Attack vector ATT&CK TA0011 Command and Control via T1071 Application Layer Protocol; T1568 Dynamic Resolution; T1041 Exfiltration Over C2 Channel; T1567 Exfiltration Over Web Service.
Prerequisites Egress proxy or NGFW with TLS-inspection or SNI-based filtering. DNS infrastructure capable of ingesting block-lists. Threat-intelligence feed (commercial or community: Spamhaus, abuse.ch, internal feeds).

Default-deny outbound is the ideal but rarely achievable in production banking environments without breaking applications. The pragmatic alternative is block-listing of known-malicious infrastructure at DNS and egress proxy, combined with anomaly detection on outbound traffic patterns from server populations that should not be making arbitrary outbound calls.

Server populations (database servers, application servers, internal middleware) typically have very narrow legitimate outbound destinations. Any deviation is signal. Workstation populations are noisier but still benefit from category-based blocking (newly-registered domains, dynamic DNS providers, file-sharing services that are not corporate-sanctioned).

Technical detail

Server-segment policy:

  • Outbound: explicit allow-list of destinations per application (vendor APIs, monitoring, package mirrors). Deny everything else.

  • Alert on any non-DNS outbound traffic from database servers.

  • Alert on outbound to newly-registered domains (NRD) - domain age < 30 days.

Workstation-segment policy:

  • Block: NRD, dynamic DNS providers, anonymisation services (Tor, public VPN), unsanctioned file-sharing.

  • Inspect: TLS inspection on all outbound except defined privacy-sensitive destinations (banking, healthcare). Document the exclusions in policy.

  • Detect: DNS query patterns characteristic of beaconing - long random subdomain labels, queries to a single domain at near-perfect periodicity.

Three holes that SNI-and-DNS-based filtering does not close, and that should be addressed in the same policy:

  • DNS-over-HTTPS (DoH). A workstation that resolves through 1.1.1.1, 8.8.8.8, Quad9, or any third-party DoH endpoint bypasses the corporate resolver entirely. Force all DNS to internal resolvers via DHCP and policy; block known DoH provider IP ranges at egress; deny outbound TCP/443 and UDP/443 to those endpoints from workstation segments.

  • QUIC (UDP/443). SNI inspection is a TCP-layer technique; QUIC slips past it. Deny outbound UDP/443 from workstations except where explicitly allow-listed (e.g. specific HTTP/3-required SaaS), and force TCP/443 fallback for everything else.

  • Direct-to-IP HTTPS. Traffic that never resolves through the corporate resolver and connects straight to an IP literal will not be caught by DNS sinkholing. Deny direct-to-IP outbound :443 from workstations - if a destination cannot present a valid hostname through the proxy, it does not get out.

S-10 Critical-asset patch tabletop and dry-run

Low

4 weeks
Risk covered When a P0 vulnerability is disclosed, the time spent rediscovering basic facts (which assets, who owns them, what the rollback procedure is) tends to dominate the response. Dry-running this work in advance is the simplest way to take that time off the critical path on the day.
Attack vector Same as S-01 / S-02. This measure is the rehearsal of those measures' response paths.
Prerequisites S-01 and S-02 in place. CMDB with application-to-infrastructure mapping. List of top internet-exposed application stacks (typically 20-40 systems).

Run a structured tabletop covering a realistic P0 scenario (see Scenario A in earlier strategic documents) for the top internet-exposed application stacks. The output is a per-system pre-staged response plan: who owns the patch, what the rollback procedure is, what compensating control can be applied if a patch is not yet available, and what the decision criteria are for taking the system offline.

Run the tabletop with the actual on-call personnel, not their managers. Time the responses. Identify and document the actual blockers - in my experience these are usually not technical but procedural (who can authorise a customer-impacting change at 03:00, who notifies the regulator).

S-11 Service-account inventory and rotation programme launch

Medium

3-6 months for full inventory; rotation is ongoing
Risk covered Long-lived, broadly-permissioned, and forgotten service accounts are a primary lateral-movement target. They typically lack MFA, have weak or unrotated credentials, and are excluded from session-monitoring controls applied to interactive logins.
Attack vector ATT&CK T1078.002 Valid Accounts: Domain Accounts; T1078.004 Cloud Accounts; T1098 Account Manipulation.
Prerequisites Directory services (AD, Entra ID, Okta) with audit logging. CMDB with service ownership. Vault platform for credential storage (HashiCorp Vault, CyberArk Conjur, cloud-native KMS).

Build and maintain an authoritative inventory of all non-human identities. For each, establish: owner, purpose, scope of access, credential type, last rotation, last use, attached compensating controls. Eliminate the long tail (unused, unowned, over-permissioned). Establish rotation cadence by tier.

This is sprint-scale work in inventory mode, and ongoing work in rotation mode. The most common dysfunction I've seen is that inventory is achievable but rotation is not, because applications hard-code their credentials. Two things have to happen in parallel: a short discovery sprint to size the problem, and then a longer programme to refactor how applications consume credentials so that rotation actually works.

Technical detail

Inventory data model (minimum):

  • Account name, type (user-as-service, managed identity, certificate-based, OAuth client).

  • Owning team, technical contact, business application.

  • Permissions held - group memberships, role assignments, IAM policies, scopes.

  • Credential metadata - last rotation, expiry, vault location.

  • Recent activity - last successful auth, source IP distribution, behaviour profile.

Eliminate first, rotate second. A reduced inventory is much easier to rotate. Target removal: any account with no successful authentication in 90 days that is not covered by a documented exception.

S-12 AI-augmented adversary scenarios in current TLPT cycle

Medium

Within current TLPT timetable
Risk covered Internal red-team and external TLPT exercises that were designed against pre-2026 adversary capability now systematically under-test the actual threat. Mis-aligned findings drive mis-aligned remediation.
Attack vector Cross-cutting - the exercise is the diagnostic, not the control.
Prerequisites Active TLPT cycle (TIBER-EU, CBEST, or local equivalent). Threat Intelligence Provider engaged. Control team established.

In coordination with the appointed Threat Intelligence Provider, refresh the Targeted Threat Intelligence Report to explicitly include AI-augmented adversary TTPs: rapid vulnerability discovery in public-facing custom code, multi-language phishing at scale, voice/deepfake-augmented social engineering, autonomous lateral movement via chained low-severity findings.

The TLPT cycle output should produce actionable findings against the specific measures in this article. If the report says "the bank is broadly resilient" without identifying detection-coverage gaps in the kill chain stages most accelerated by AI assistance, the test was too soft.

5. Medium term (6-18 months)

Eleven measures. These are programme-scale efforts that industrialise and extend the short-term work. Most need coordination across several engineering teams, dedicated programme management, and incremental tooling investment. They build directly on the short-term measures, and in most cases they depend on them - trying to start here without first having done the short-term work is a familiar way to spend a lot of money slowly.

M-01 Continuous attack-surface management with automated revalidation

Medium

6-9 months
Risk covered S-03 closes the attack-surface gap once; M-01 keeps it closed. Without continuous revalidation, surface drift returns the gap to its original size within months as the estate evolves.
Attack vector Same as S-03 - TA0043 Reconnaissance, TA0001 Initial Access.
Prerequisites S-03 completed. EASM platform tendered or extended to continuous mode. Integration with CMDB and ticketing.

Move external attack-surface management from a one-time discovery exercise to a daily-cadence continuous capability with auto-ticketing of unmatched findings. Integrate with the cloud-tenant inventory so that newly-provisioned cloud resources appear in the EASM view within hours, not weeks.

Pair with internal attack-surface management (continuous network discovery, identity attack-path analysis with tools such as BloodHound Enterprise, Netwrix Privilege Secure) to cover the inside-the-perimeter equivalent.

Technical detail

Integration points:

  • Cloud provider APIs - pull the authoritative inventory of public IPs, load balancers, public buckets, public-facing functions every 4 hours.

  • DNS - monitor zone changes; alert on new external-facing names.

  • Certificate transparency logs - monitor for new certificates issued for owned domains; this is the leading indicator of new exposed services.

  • Internal AD/Entra - identity attack-path graph computed weekly with diffs flagged.

M-02 SBOM-driven vulnerability response for first-party software

High

9-15 months
Risk covered When a critical vulnerability is disclosed in a widely-used open-source component, the slowest part of the response is usually identifying which internal applications actually use it. Without SBOMs, that is days of manual investigation. With SBOMs, it is a query.
Attack vector ATT&CK T1190 Exploit Public-Facing Application - specifically when the exploit is in a transitive dependency.
Prerequisites CI/CD pipeline integration permission. SBOM tooling (Anchore, Snyk, Sonatype, GitHub Advanced Security, or open-source Syft/Grype). Centralised SBOM repository. CVE feed integration.

Produce a Software Bill of Materials for every internally-developed application at build time, in CycloneDX or SPDX format. Store SBOMs centrally with build provenance. On CVE disclosure, query the SBOM repository to identify all affected applications within minutes.

This is high-complexity because it depends on CI/CD discipline that many enterprises have never consistently enforced. Most of the actual work is not technical at all - it is convincing every development team to integrate the SBOM step, then validating the output, then gating release on SBOM completeness. None of those things is hard individually, but doing all three across an estate of dozens or hundreds of teams is a multi-quarter exercise in organisational persuasion.

Technical detail

Pipeline integration pattern (illustrative for Java / Node / Python / Go):

# In CI, after build:

syft packages dir:./build -o cyclonedx-json > sbom.cdx.json

# Push to central SBOM store keyed on application + version + commit:

curl -X POST -H "Authorization: Bearer $TOKEN" \

--data-binary @sbom.cdx.json \

https://sbom-store.internal/v1/sbom/$APP/$VERSION

# On CVE alert, query the store:

curl https://sbom-store.internal/v1/affected?cve=CVE-2026-XXXXX

→ list of all (app, version) pairs containing the vulnerable component

For commercial third-party software, demand SBOMs as a procurement requirement at next renewal (covered separately in M-09).

A note on practical reality: most large banks carry non-trivial fractions of their estate that pre-date pipeline-as-code and cannot trivially produce SBOMs. The pragmatic answer is to triage - prioritise SBOM coverage for internet-facing and high-criticality applications, accept partial coverage on the rest, and track completeness as a programme metric over time.

M-03 Microsegmentation of Tier-0/Tier-1 systems

High

12-18 months
Risk covered Lateral movement between systems on the same network segment requires no exploit at all - it is the assumed baseline once any single host is compromised. With AI-augmented adversaries automating lateral movement, free traversal between the perimeter and the data-bearing core becomes the dividing line between an incident you can contain and a breach you have to disclose.
Attack vector ATT&CK TA0008 Lateral Movement broadly, with focus on T1021 Remote Services, T1570 Lateral Tool Transfer, T1210 Exploitation of Remote Services.
Prerequisites Microsegmentation platform (Illumio, Akamai Guardicore, Cisco Secure Workload, native cloud network policies). Application dependency mapping. Sufficient operational maturity to handle policy-induced application breakage during rollout.

Implement default-deny lateral connectivity for Tier-0 (identity, KMS, CA) and Tier-1 (production application infrastructure) systems. Allow only explicitly defined east-west flows. The rest of the estate moves to microsegmentation in the long term (L-02); the medium-term effort is concentrated on the systems that matter most.

Of all the short and medium term measures in this article, this one is by far the most demanding to deliver. The technology has been mature for years - the difficulty is operational. Application dependency discovery is iterative, policy errors break applications, and rollback procedures need to be rehearsed before they are used in anger. The team will need real authority and air cover to keep going through the inevitable production incidents during rollout, and senior sponsors who do not lose their nerve the first time something breaks.

Technical detail

Sequencing:

  • Phase 1 - discovery only. Deploy in observe mode for 4-8 weeks per workload group. Collect every flow.

  • Phase 2 - author policies in dry-run. Compare predicted blocks against observed traffic. Iterate.

  • Phase 3 - enforcement on a single low-stakes Tier-1 application as canary. 30 days of monitoring.

  • Phase 4 - staged rollout. Group applications by criticality, business hours, and team support availability. Roll forward only when the previous group is stable.

Special-case Tier-0: identity and key-management infrastructure can typically be segmented quickly because they have well-known, narrow communication patterns. Do these first as an early win.

M-04 Service-account credential rotation industrialisation

High

12-18 months
Risk covered S-11 inventories the problem. M-04 fixes it. Without M-04, service-account credentials remain a multi-year-old, shared, vault-extracted-once-and-cached liability.
Attack vector Same as S-11 - T1078.002, T1078.004, T1098.
Prerequisites S-11 substantially complete. Vault platform deployed with broker capability. Application teams resourced for credential-consumption refactoring.

Migrate service-account credential consumption from "vault dispenses, application caches forever" to short-lived, dynamically-issued credentials. The pattern depends on platform - for cloud-native workloads, use cloud-IAM workload identity (IRSA on AWS, workload identity on GCP, managed identities on Azure). For on-premise, use Vault dynamic secrets, Kerberos with short-TTL tickets, or X.509 certificate-based service authentication with short rotation.

The work is overwhelmingly on the application side: every application that consumes a service-account credential needs to be refactored to fetch on demand and to tolerate rotation. This is multi-team, multi-quarter work, and in most institutions it ends up being the slowest piece of the wider IAM modernisation effort. Running M-03 and M-04 in parallel is a serious test of organisational change capacity; sequence them deliberately rather than just running them concurrently because the calendar permits it.

Technical detail

Target patterns by environment:

  • AWS workloads: IRSA / IAM Roles for Service Accounts; pod-level IAM credentials with no long-lived secret.

  • Azure workloads: Managed Identities; no client secrets in code or config.

  • Kubernetes generally: SPIFFE/SPIRE for workload identity, short-lived SVIDs.

  • On-premise application-to-database: Vault Database Secrets Engine issues credentials with TTL of minutes; application fetches per connection-pool refresh.

  • Service-to-service mTLS: short-lived certificates from internal CA, rotation cadence of hours not months.

M-05 Detection-engineering programme: behavioural rules and ATT&CK coverage

Medium

Continuous; initial maturity 9-12 months
Risk covered Signature- and IOC-based detection degrades quickly against AI-augmented adversaries that can mass-produce previously-unseen artefacts. Behavioural detection - based on what an attacker has to do, not what they happen to use - degrades much more slowly.
Attack vector Cross-cutting. The output of this measure is detection capability against most ATT&CK tactics, not a specific defence against one.
Prerequisites SIEM with adequate retention (90 days hot, 1+ year warm). Detection-engineering function (separate from SOC analysts; this is a development discipline, not a triage role). MITRE ATT&CK Navigator. Test infrastructure for detection-rule development (Atomic Red Team, Caldera, or commercial purple-team platform).

Establish or scale a detection-engineering practice that produces, tests, deploys, and retires detections on a development-team cadence. Organise rule coverage against MITRE ATT&CK to expose blind spots. Rules are versioned in Git, tested against generated adversary behaviour, and have explicit signal/noise contracts (false-positive rate budget, severity, response playbook reference).

This is the core defensive engineering investment of the medium term. It does not replace the SIEM platform - it changes how detection content gets built and maintained. What matters is not how many rules you have, but how much of the ATT&CK landscape they actually cover with defensible signal-to-noise.

Technical detail

Detection-as-code workflow:

  • Rules written in Sigma (vendor-neutral) or native SIEM language; versioned in Git.

  • Each rule has metadata: ATT&CK technique(s), data source dependencies, expected event volume, false-positive budget, response playbook ID.

  • Pre-deployment: simulate against a corpus of historical events for false-positive estimation.

  • Post-deployment: weekly review of triggered rules; rules exceeding FP budget are tuned, parked, or retired.

A few behavioural detections worth prioritising (these all fire on stages of an AI-augmented kill chain):

  • Authentication primitive abuse - Kerberoasting (T1558.003), AS-REP roasting (T1558.004), Golden/Silver Ticket (T1558.001/.002), DCSync (T1003.006).

  • Process injection lineage - code creating threads in remote processes (T1055), particularly LSASS access patterns from non-MSS processes (T1003.001).

  • Credential dump heuristics - readers of NTDS.dit, SAM hive access by non-system processes.

  • Living-off-the-land - abnormal sequences of LOLBINs (rundll32, regsvr32, mshta, certutil, bitsadmin) particularly with network egress.

  • Cloud-control-plane recon - bulk enumerate-style API calls (List*, Describe*) from new principals.

  • Beaconing - periodic outbound connections with low jitter, particularly from server populations.

M-06 Deception infrastructure (canary tokens and decoy assets)

Medium

6-9 months
Risk covered An adversary in the environment must perform reconnaissance. Deception assets ensure that reconnaissance produces high-fidelity alerts at zero false-positive rate, materially shortening time-to-detect for the discovery phase of an operation.
Attack vector ATT&CK TA0007 Discovery broadly - every technique in this tactic is a deception trigger by design.
Prerequisites Network access to deploy decoys in monitoring-significant network segments. Naming conventions that pass casual inspection (decoys must not be obviously named DECOY-01). SIEM integration for canary triggers.

Two complementary capabilities. First, canary tokens - fake credentials, files, AWS keys, mailboxes, document stubs - embedded in legitimate-looking locations across the estate. Any access to a canary is, by construction, unauthorised activity. Second, decoy assets - full or partial fake hosts, services, or shares dropped into network segments where adversary reconnaissance is likely to land. Commercial offerings (Thinkst Canary, Commvault Threatwise, SentinelOne Singularity Hologram) and open-source equivalents (Canarytokens, Honeyd) both work; the cost is mostly deployment effort, not licensing.

Deception is one of the few defensive investments where the false-positive rate is structurally near zero - which is why I keep coming back to it. The hard parts are convincing the SOC to actually take canary alerts seriously, and keeping the decoys plausible as the legitimate environment changes around them.

Technical detail

Canary token placements with high signal value:

  • Fake AWS keys named like real ones ("prod-data-export-readonly") in developer workstation .aws/credentials files.

  • Honeydocs (Word/PDF) named "Network Diagram - Q4 2026.docx" or "Privileged Account Vault Backup.xlsx" on file shares with relaxed permissions.

  • Service-account credentials in scripts/configs that are never legitimately consumed but look extracted from a real one.

  • DNS records for plausible-but-fake internal hosts (internal-vault, scm-backup, payroll-archive) that pass DNS but have no service.

  • Synthetic credentials committed to internal repositories and "removed" in a later commit, but still recoverable through git log -p. Trivial to find for an attacker (or AI-assisted code scanner) doing repository reconnaissance, near-zero false-positive rate, and high signal for repository compromise or insider recon. Particularly worth deploying in M&A diligence repositories given the targeting pattern flagged in S-04.

Decoy hosts: deploy at least one decoy per significant network segment. Match the OS and service mix of the legitimate hosts in that segment. Run minimal services to reduce maintenance burden but enough to engage automated reconnaissance.

M-07 AI-assisted SOC triage and analyst augmentation

Medium

9-12 months
Risk covered The pace of the adversary is now ahead of analyst capacity. Without augmentation, the SOC itself becomes the slowest part of the response - the difference between detection and contained incident is often measured in the minutes an analyst spends pivoting between consoles.
Attack vector Cross-cutting - this is a defensive operations enhancement, not a control.
Prerequisites Internal AI use approved by the institution's AI Risk and Privacy functions. SIEM/SOAR with API access. Defined data-handling boundary (no customer data into external models without contracted controls). Detection-engineering practice (M-05) producing structured rule metadata.

Deploy frontier-model assistance to specific SOC workflows: alert summarisation across telemetry sources, evidence aggregation for triage, suggested next-pivot, and draft IR write-up. Do not delegate response decisions to the model. The pattern is augmentation of human analysts, with model output framed as suggestion and the human as decision-maker.

Two patterns to avoid. The first is a thin chat-interface wrapper that does not integrate with the actual SOC tooling, ends up being a curiosity, and is then quietly abandoned after six months. The second is a deeply integrated automation that takes containment actions on model output without human review, eventually decides confidently to do something stupid in production, and burns the credibility of every other AI-defence proposal you make for the next two years.

Technical detail

Suitable initial use cases (high value, low risk):

  • Alert summarisation - given an alert and the past 24h of related events, produce a concise narrative for the analyst's first read.

  • Detection rule explanation - given a triggered Sigma/SIEM rule and recent triggers, explain the logic in natural language for a junior analyst.

  • IR documentation - draft a timeline of events from raw logs for the analyst to review and correct.

  • Code review of detection rules - flag logic errors, missing edge cases, suspected false-positive patterns.

Use cases to defer until the platform is stable:

  • Autonomous containment actions (isolate host, disable account) without human approval. Hard rule: human approves every containment action involving production systems.

  • Customer-facing incident communications drafted by model. Sensitive - needs Communications/Legal review pattern that does not yet exist for AI-generated content.

Data-handling - agree explicit boundaries with the Privacy and AI Risk functions. Frontier models hosted in the institution's own cloud tenant with contractual data-handling guarantees (Bedrock, Vertex AI, Azure OpenAI in the appropriate region) are typically the acceptable surface; consumer-grade APIs are not.

Treat the SOC LLM as an attack surface in its own right. The pipelines feeding it - log lines, alert payloads, file names, process command lines, DNS labels, User-Agent strings - contain attacker-controlled fields. An adversary who knows the bank uses LLM-assisted triage can embed prompt-injection payloads in any of these to instruct the model to suppress an alert, summarise it innocuously, or recommend the wrong pivot. This is not theoretical and has been demonstrated against commercial products. The mitigation set:

  • Treat any model input derived from attacker-controlled fields as untrusted by default. Quarantine and quote-escape such fields before they reach the prompt.

  • Enforce structured output schemas (the model returns JSON with named fields, not free text). This reduces, but does not eliminate, the surface.

  • The analyst reviews the raw alert, not just the model summary, before any containment decision. The model produces a triage hypothesis, not a verdict.

  • Log every model input and output for retrospective audit. Periodically sample for evidence of injection attempts.

L-04 (bounded autonomous response) inherits this concern in amplified form. If the model can be steered, its verdicts cannot be trusted as a basis for autonomous action without independent corroboration from a non-LLM signal.

M-08 Pre-staged compensating controls for unpatchable conditions

Medium

6-9 months
Risk covered Some assets cannot be patched on the timescales S-01 demands - vendor-controlled appliances, certified medical or industrial systems, end-of-life infrastructure pending migration. For these, compensating control must be pre-staged so it can be applied within the patch SLA window.
Attack vector Same as S-01 - T1190 and adjacent.
Prerequisites S-01 SLA tiering. Asset inventory with patch-feasibility flag. WAF / NGFW capable of virtual patching. Network segmentation capability for emergency isolation.

For each system marked patch-infeasible-in-SLA, document a pre-staged compensating control that can be applied at P0/P1 without further design. Examples: virtual patch rule on the WAF, ACL change at the segment boundary, complete network isolation with documented business-impact, application-layer mitigation by configuration change.

What this produces is a per-system playbook held by the SOC, reviewed quarterly and dry-run annually. The point is straightforward: when a P0 disclosure lands, the response time for an unpatchable system should be no worse than for a patchable one, because you are applying a pre-designed mitigation rather than designing one in real time.

Technical detail

Compensating-control catalogue (per system, illustrative):

  • WAF rule template - for HTTP-exposed applications, define which signatures, request properties, or path patterns can be blocked under emergency conditions and what user impact this produces.

  • Segment ACL change - for backend systems, define the smallest reachable-set reduction that preserves required function.

  • Authentication step-up - for IdP-fronted applications, enforce additional MFA challenge or block specific risky sign-in conditions.

  • Service degradation - for systems where partial unavailability is preferable to compromise, document the degradation path (read-only mode, feature disable, geographic restriction).

M-09 Third-party software: SBOM-and-patch-cadence procurement requirements

Medium

Aligned to contract renewal cycles (12-24 months for full pass)
Risk covered First-party SBOM coverage (M-02) does nothing for vulnerabilities in third-party closed-source software, which is typically the larger share of the application estate. Procurement language is one of the few things that actually has teeth here.
Attack vector T1190 in the supply-chain version: a third-party product the institution consumes is the vulnerable component.
Prerequisites Procurement function aligned. Legal review of contract language. Identification of critical third-party software (overlap with DORA's critical ICT third-party list where applicable).

Add to procurement standard terms, applied at next renewal: (1) vendor produces and shares SBOMs in a machine-readable format on each release; (2) vendor commits to maximum patch cadence for critical vulnerabilities (target: 7 days for KEV-listed); (3) vendor notifies the customer of any active exploitation of their product within a defined window; (4) vendor permits or enables compensating controls (e.g., does not contractually forbid WAF rules in front of the product).

This is procurement and legal work, not engineering work. The cost is mostly internal effort, plus some negotiating ground given up to critical vendors who can refuse the terms. For vendors who do refuse, the fallback is to document the residual risk explicitly and apply compensating controls structurally.

M-10 Recovery rehearsal: ransomware-grade incident drill against tier-1 application

High

Quarterly thereafter; first end-to-end run 6-9 months
Risk covered Recovery capability that has never been exercised end-to-end is unproven. From what I have seen, and from the post-incident reports of major recent breaches that have been published (Norsk Hydro, MGM, Change Healthcare, among others), actual recovery time tends to run several multiples longer than the figure documented in the BCP. AI-augmented adversaries do not change this directly; they increase the probability that you will need to use it.
Attack vector Cross-cutting - this measure exercises response to all of the above.
Prerequisites Documented DR procedures for the target application. Backup integrity work (M-11) substantially complete - if it is not, the drill turns into a restore-engineering exercise rather than a recovery rehearsal. Cross-team coordination - IT, application owner, business, communications, legal.

Run an end-to-end recovery drill against one Tier-1 application, treating it as if compromised by ransomware-class destructive attack. Activate backup recovery, parallel rebuild, customer communications, and supervisory notification. Time every step. The output is a remediation list of every assumption that proved incorrect.

The drill must be unannounced to the application owner team beyond the date window. Telegraphed drills produce theatrical results. Run with the actual on-call personnel. Senior leadership is in scope - communications drafts require their actual review.

Technical detail

Required telemetry from the drill:

  • Time from incident declaration to first containment action.

  • Time from declaration to authoritative scope determination.

  • Time from declaration to backup integrity confirmation.

  • Time from declaration to first restored service for a defined success criterion.

  • Time from declaration to customer-facing communication issued.

  • Number of decisions that required escalation to a person not reachable on first attempt.

M-11 Backup immutability and recovery-path validation

High

9-12 months
Risk covered M-10 (recovery rehearsal) treats backup integrity as a prerequisite. In practice, at most institutions, that work is partially done at best - backups exist, but immutability and air-gap claims have not been independently tested under the assumption that the backup infrastructure itself is in scope of the attack. A ransomware-class adversary who has been resident long enough to know the environment will go after the backups first; recovery is then catastrophic, not routine.
Attack vector ATT&CK T1490 Inhibit System Recovery; T1485 Data Destruction; T1486 Data Encrypted for Impact (where applied to backup volumes).
Prerequisites Existing backup platform with WORM / object-lock capability or air-gap option (Veeam, Rubrik, Cohesity, Commvault, native cloud object-lock). Asset inventory mapping production workloads to backup policies. Runbook for emergency restore from the air-gapped tier.

Enforce immutability or true air-gap for the recovery copy of every Tier-0 and Tier-1 system. The objective is that no operationally-resident credential, including those of the backup-administration role, can delete or encrypt the recovery tier within its retention window. Validate this independently rather than relying on the platform's marketing.

The validation step is what most institutions skip and is the part that matters. Pick a representative system, follow the documented restore path end-to-end, in a clean environment, against a backup taken before a simulated compromise window. Time it. Capture every dependency that turns out to be missing, every credential that is no longer valid, every license check that fails because the original tenant is offline. The output is a remediation list - and, separately, a realistic RTO that should be reflected in the BCP.

Technical detail

Minimum control set:

  • Immutability enforced at the storage layer (S3 Object Lock in compliance mode, Azure immutable blobs with legal-hold, or equivalent on-prem). Retention longer than the worst-case dwell time (90 days minimum).

  • Backup admin credentials in a separate identity domain from production admin credentials. A compromise of production AD must not be able to authenticate to the backup tenant.

  • Air-gapped tier (cold offline copy) for the most critical 5-10 systems, restored from independently at least annually.

  • Restore validation - automated test restore of representative systems on a defined cadence (weekly for top tier, monthly for the rest), with the restored system actually booted and a smoke test executed against it.

6. Long term (18+ months)

Six measures, all of them architectural in nature. These are changes that take quarters to design and years to land. Listing something here is not a commitment to do it - it is a flag that the work needs evaluation and, if pursued, programme-grade governance.

L-01 Identity-centric zero-trust architecture across the application estate

Very High

24-36 months
Risk covered Network-position-based trust is the structural source of most lateral-movement opportunities. A zero-trust architecture moves the trust decision to identity + device + session context evaluated per request, eliminating implicit trust granted by network location.
Attack vector Comprehensively reduces leverage from TA0008 Lateral Movement, TA0001 Initial Access (post-perimeter-bypass scenarios), and TA0006 Credential Access (theft from privileged-network-position assumptions).
Prerequisites Mature IdP with conditional access (S-06, S-08 deployed). Short-lived workload credentials (M-04) - long-lived shared service credentials defeat per-request trust evaluation. Service-mesh capability for east-west traffic (Istio, Linkerd, Cilium Service Mesh, native cloud equivalents such as Amazon VPC Lattice or Azure Service Fabric Mesh). Identity-aware proxy capability for HTTP traffic (Google IAP, Cloudflare Access, Zscaler Private Access, native cloud equivalents).

Replace the existing perimeter-and-VPN model with a zero-trust architecture where every application access is authenticated, authorised, and continuously evaluated against device posture and session risk, regardless of network origin. East-west traffic is encrypted and authenticated via service mesh. Application access is brokered through identity-aware proxies, eliminating wide network exposure for the bulk of internal applications.

This is a multi-year programme of work. It is partially a continuation of microsegmentation (M-03) but extends to the application access plane. The reason it is here and not in the medium term is the breadth: every internal application is in scope, each with its own dependencies, identity model, and operational team.

L-02 Microsegmentation extended to the full estate

Very High

24-36 months
Risk covered M-03 covers the most important systems. Extending to the rest of the estate is what eliminates the long tail of unrestricted east-west connectivity that, in current architectures, makes lateral movement near-frictionless once any host is compromised.
Attack vector Same as M-03 - TA0008 Lateral Movement, T1021, T1570, T1210.
Prerequisites M-03 substantially complete. Operational maturity demonstrated at Tier-0/Tier-1 scale.

Extend the microsegmentation programme from the Tier-0/Tier-1 systems addressed in M-03 to the rest of the production estate. The technology and operational pattern are unchanged; the cost is breadth and the friction is application owner engagement at scale.

Scope explicitly in the long term because the operational learning curve from M-03 takes 12-18 months to consolidate before the team is ready to scale the pattern to dozens of additional application groups. Doing this work too early - before the operational pattern is proven - produces high failure rates and the resulting credibility damage stalls the programme for years.

L-03 Detection coverage maturity to assessed parity with adversary kill chains

Very High

24-36 months continuous
Risk covered M-05 establishes the engineering practice. L-03 is the destination state: comprehensive detection coverage measured against an authoritative adversary model, with continuous validation that controls trigger as expected.
Attack vector Cross-cutting.
Prerequisites M-05 established and producing detection-as-code at engineering velocity. Purple-teaming function operational. Sufficient data lake (≥18 months retention) for retrospective hunt and detection backtesting.

Maturity destination for the detection programme: every ATT&CK technique relevant to the institution's environment has at least one detection of documented signal-to-noise quality. Purple-team exercises run continuously, validating that controls fire as expected and that gaps are addressed at engineering velocity. Rule count is the wrong way to think about success; what counts is technique coverage with validated detection efficacy.

A note on what is realistically achievable: even mature programmes do not reach 100% ATT&CK coverage. In my experience the curve tends to flatten somewhere in the 60-70% range, with the remaining techniques being either low relevance to the environment or genuinely undetectable with current telemetry. Document the irreducible gaps and address them through structural changes - telemetry investment, control replacement - rather than by writing more detection content.

L-04 AI-on-AI defence: autonomous response with bounded action authority

Very High

24+ months; conservative deployment over years
Risk covered Adversary speed exceeds human-in-the-loop response capacity for some action classes. Bounded autonomous response - narrow, well-defined containment actions taken without human approval within pre-defined safety constraints - closes the speed gap for the worst cases without introducing operational risk through over-broad automation.
Attack vector Defensive - addresses the response-pace asymmetry across all attack vectors.
Prerequisites M-07 mature. Detection-engineering function (M-05) with high-confidence rule classification. Documented action-authority model with explicit safety boundaries. Senior IT and Risk approval. Comprehensive audit trail for every autonomous action.

Define a small, well-bounded set of containment actions that can be taken autonomously on high-confidence detections without human approval. Examples: isolating a non-production endpoint, disabling a non-privileged user session, blocking a specific outbound destination at the proxy. The boundary is what is reversible and low-blast-radius.

This measure carries operational risk and has to be built carefully. The basic loop looks roughly like this: a detection produces a high-confidence verdict with structured metadata, the automation checks that verdict against pre-defined safety constraints (asset criticality, time of day, business impact), and if everything is within bounds the action executes with a human notified after the fact. Anything outside bounds escalates to a human for approval before anything happens. Every autonomous action is fully logged and reviewable, and the system must include an emergency stop that disables all autonomy on operator command - because the day you need that switch will not be a day on which you have time to build it.

Technical detail

Action-authority boundaries to consider (illustrative):

  • Permitted autonomous: isolating a workstation in a non-production segment on confirmed C2 detection; revoking a session token on confirmed token-theft detection; blocking a specific outbound IP on confirmed C2.

  • Always human-approved: any action affecting customer-facing services; any account disable for a privileged user; any production-server isolation; any cloud-tenant-wide control change.

  • Forbidden: autonomous offensive action (counter-attack, retaliatory traffic). Defensive only.

Very High

Aligned to broader IT modernisation roadmap
Risk covered Some legacy systems cannot meet the cyber posture this threat model demands, and no amount of compensating control will bring them up to it. At that point the only honest answer is to replace them.
Attack vector Eliminates technical debt that perpetuates exposures across multiple ATT&CK tactics.
Prerequisites Existing IT modernisation programme. Identification of the specific systems concerned. Business engagement on functional replacement.

Identify the specific legacy systems that are the locus of the highest residual risk after compensating controls have been applied (M-08), and integrate their replacement into the IT modernisation roadmap with cyber-posture as an explicit prioritisation criterion. This is not a security-led programme; it is a security input into the existing modernisation prioritisation process.

Frontier-AI-driven threat acceleration sharpens the priority of replacement for some specific systems but does not change the cost/benefit fundamentals of the broader modernisation programme.

8. Two things I keep coming back to

Most of what is in this article is technical and procedural. I want to close on two beliefs that are neither, but that I think shape whether the technical and procedural work succeeds or fails.

8.1 The best defence against an AI attacker is mastering AI defence

This is the thesis behind a lot of what is in the medium and long term sections, and it is worth stating plainly. The pace, scale, and parallelism that frontier AI gives to attackers cannot be matched by human defenders alone. We should not pretend otherwise. The institutions that come out of this period in the strongest position will be the ones that learn to apply frontier AI on the defensive side at least as competently as their adversaries are applying it on the offensive side - in detection, in triage, in vulnerability discovery, in red teaming, eventually in bounded autonomous response (L-04). That is not an abdication of human judgement. It is an honest read of where the asymmetry is, and the only realistic way I see of closing it.

8.2 The knowledge is already in the technical teams; management has to make it safe to surface

In every organisation I have worked with, there is a striking amount of awareness within the engineering, SOC, and infrastructure teams about exactly where the weak points are. People know which systems are out of patch cycle, which controls are bypassed in practice, which alerts get ignored, which compensating controls only exist on paper. What changes from one organisation to another is whether that knowledge surfaces upwards proactively, or whether it stays buried until an incident drags it out under unflattering circumstances.

This is not a technical problem. It is a management one. The engineers will tell you what is wrong - if the cultural signal they get back is recognition rather than blame, and if they trust that raising an uncomfortable issue will improve their standing rather than damage it. Conversely, in environments where bad news is a career risk, the bad news goes underground, and you find out about it after the breach. None of the technical measures in this article will land properly without that cultural baseline. I would go further and say that, in my experience, the gap between organisations that handle AI-augmented threats well and ones that do not will be explained more by this management posture than by any single technical investment.

I should add, in fairness, that I am fortunate to currently work within an organisation where this is genuinely taken seriously at senior level - and the contrast with what I have seen elsewhere is, in itself, evidence that the cultural posture is a choice, not an inevitability.

Annex A - Detection-engineering patterns

The patterns below are illustrative, not exhaustive. They are written in pseudo-Sigma form. Field names are normalised to common ECS/Sigma conventions; translate to your SIEM's native schema. Each pattern includes the ATT&CK technique it covers, the data source required, and a note on signal/noise considerations.

A.1 EDR tamper detection (T1562.001)

Fires on EDR agent service stop, uninstall, or telemetry-stream interruption.

title: EDR Agent Service Stopped or Uninstalled

logsource:

product: windows

service: system

detection:

selection_event:

EventID: 7036

ServiceName|contains:

- 'CSFalconService' # CrowdStrike

- 'SentinelAgent' # SentinelOne

- 'WinDefend' # Defender

- 'MsMpEng' # Defender engine

State: 'stopped'

selection_uninstall:

EventID: 1033 # MsiInstaller - install/uninstall (with action code)

Product|contains:

- 'CrowdStrike Falcon'

- 'SentinelOne Agent'

selection_appremove:

EventID: 11724 # Application removed (Windows Installer)

condition: selection_event or selection_uninstall or selection_appremove

level: high

tags:

- attack.defense_evasion

- attack.t1562.001

A few caveats. The list of service and product names has to be maintained against the actual EDR estate. Pair this rule with a counterpart on the EDR cloud console - one that alerts when an endpoint's last-checkin exceeds a tunable threshold. Bidirectional detection covers the case where a host stops reporting because it is genuinely offline (legitimate), versus the case where the host stops reporting because the agent has been killed (suspicious).

A.3 Beaconing detection (T1071, T1568.002)

# Detect outbound connections with low jitter, characteristic of C2 beacons.

# Run as scheduled query against proxy/firewall logs (e.g., every 30 minutes).

events

| where event.kind == 'network' and direction == 'outbound'

| where source.ip in (server_segment_cidrs) # apply scope

| bin time = bin(@timestamp, 1m)

| summarize

connections = count(),

distinct_dst = dcount(destination.domain),

intervals = make_list(time)

by source.ip, destination.domain

| extend interval_stddev = series_stddev(intervals_to_diffs(intervals))

| where connections >= 12 # at least 12 events in window

| where interval_stddev < 5 # low jitter

| where distinct_dst <= 2 # narrow set of destinations

| project source.ip, destination.domain, connections, interval_stddev

On false positives. The usual suspects are legitimate periodic traffic - software update checks, monitoring beacons, NTP. Build an allow-list before you deploy. Scoping the rule to server segments first dramatically reduces noise compared to applying it across the workstation estate.

logsource:

A.6 AI-specific patterns (ATT&CK v19 T1682, T1683)

ATT&CK v19 added two technique IDs that capture adversary use of AI services directly: T1682 Query Public AI Services and T1683 Generate Content. The patterns below are starting points for these and for prompt injection of internal AI tooling - none of them is a fully-tuned production rule, and each will need calibration against the legitimate AI-use baseline in the institution. Treat them as a place to begin, not as drop-in content.

Pattern 6.1 - bulk LLM API egress from non-developer populations. Outbound connections to public inference endpoints (api.openai.com, api.anthropic.com, generativelanguage.googleapis.com, and the major Bedrock and Vertex regional endpoints) from server populations or from workstation populations that are not authorised AI-tooling users. Tune against the institution's approved-AI-tools allow-list. False-positive sources include CI/CD pipelines that legitimately call inference APIs and security tools that integrate with LLM providers; both should be on the allow-list.

Pattern 6.2 - prompt-injection signal in attacker-controlled fields. Look for instruction-formatted content in fields that feed AI tooling: long Base64 blobs in User-Agent strings, "SYSTEM:" or "### Instruction" tokens in DNS labels, command lines, or process names, and unusual unicode patterns (zero-width characters, RTL overrides) in fields the SOC LLM (M-07) reads. This is the detection counterpart to the M-07 mitigation set.

Pattern 6.3 - AI-assisted credential stuffing. High-volume authentication failures distributed across many source IPs (rotated proxies) but with suspiciously homogeneous TLS fingerprints (JA3/JA4) or user-agent strings - the signature of a single tool driving many endpoints. Pre-AI credential-stuffing attacks tended to vary the user-agent randomly; AI-assisted versions tend not to bother.

Annex B - Vulnerability prioritisation reference

This annex pulls together the prioritisation logic referenced in S-01 in one place, as a quick reference for vulnerability-management engineers.

B.1 The four signals

  • CVSS - Common Vulnerability Scoring System. Measures technical severity if exploited. Range 0-10. Useful as a severity bound; insufficient as a prioritisation metric on its own. Use v3.1 base score until v4 is universally adopted by vendor advisories.

  • EPSS - Exploit Prediction Scoring System (FIRST.org). Probability that a CVE will be exploited in the next 30 days, range 0-1. Updated daily. v4 model live since March 2025. JSON API at https://api.first.org/data/v1/epss; daily CSV at https://epss.empiricalsecurity.com/.

  • KEV - Known Exploited Vulnerabilities catalogue (CISA). Confirmed in-the-wild exploitation. If a CVE is on KEV, it is being exploited; treat as P0 regardless of CVSS or EPSS. Free feed at https://www.cisa.gov/kev.

  • Asset context - Internet-exposed? Tier-0/Tier-1? Holds regulated data? Patchable in SLA? These determine where to apply each tier's threshold and whether compensating control is required (M-08).

B.2 Decision matrix

Tier Trigger condition (any) SLA (internet-exposed)
P0 - emergency On KEV; OR EPSS ≥ 0.6; OR vendor advisory references active exploitation Patch within 8h; compensating control within 4h
P1 - pre-emergency CVSS ≥ 8.0 AND EPSS ≥ 0.1, not yet KEV Patch within 24h
P2 - high CVSS ≥ 7.0 OR EPSS ≥ 0.05 Patch within 7 days
P3 - standard Below P2 thresholds Standard 30-day cycle

For non-internet-exposed assets, shift each tier by one level (P0 → P1 SLA, etc.). For Tier-0 assets (identity, KMS, CA), keep internet-exposed SLAs even if the asset is internal - the blast-radius justifies the urgency.

B.3 EPSS trend signal

Persist daily EPSS snapshots. A CVE whose EPSS doubles in a week deserves attention even if its current absolute value is moderate (e.g., from 0.02 to 0.04 in 7 days). This early-warning signal frequently precedes KEV listing by days. Surface EPSS deltas in the vulnerability dashboard alongside absolute scores.

One operational caveat. A doubling from 0.005 to 0.01 produces the same alert as 0.02 to 0.04 but with much weaker signal, and at floor values you will get noise from model jitter rather than from real movement. The pragmatic rule is: trigger only when both the doubling condition AND the new value is at or above 0.02. Below that floor, persist the trend but do not alert on it.

B.4 Caveats

  • EPSS is a model output. It can underestimate a vulnerability that is exploited in targeted operations against specific industries before broad exploitation begins. Maintain a manual override path for threat intelligence on financial-sector-targeted CVEs even if EPSS is low.

  • KEV is reactive - by the time a CVE is added, broad exploitation is already happening. Do not wait for KEV; use EPSS as the leading signal.

  • Vendor advisories sometimes precede public CVE assignment. Treat "actively exploited" language in a vendor advisory as P0 regardless of whether the CVE is yet on KEV or has an EPSS score.

Annex C - Glossary

  • AiTM - Adversary-in-the-Middle. Phishing pattern where the attacker proxies the legitimate authentication site, capturing credentials and session tokens. Defeated by phishing-resistant MFA bound to the origin domain.

  • ASM / EASM - Attack Surface Management / External Attack Surface Management. Continuous discovery of internet-exposed assets attributable to the organisation.

  • ATT&CK - MITRE adversary tactics and techniques framework. Vendor-neutral taxonomy used throughout this article for attack vector references. ATT&CK v19 (released 28 April 2026) introduced two changes that directly affect the article: the Defense Evasion tactic was split into Stealth and Defense Impairment, and several social-engineering techniques (formerly T1656 Impersonation, T1672 Email Spoofing) were re-organised under a new parent T1684 Social Engineering. Where the article cites IDs that have moved, the v18 IDs are kept and the v19 successor noted in parentheses.

  • Beacon - Periodic outbound connection from an implant to attacker infrastructure for command and control. Detected by interval analysis of outbound flows.

  • CISA KEV - US Cybersecurity and Infrastructure Security Agency Known Exploited Vulnerabilities catalogue. Free feed; daily updates.

  • CMDB - Configuration Management Database. The authoritative register of IT assets, services, ownership, and relationships referenced throughout the article as the asset inventory of record.

  • CVSS - Common Vulnerability Scoring System. Severity score from 0 to 10.

  • EDR - Endpoint Detection and Response. Agent-based endpoint security platform with behavioural detection and response capabilities.

  • EPSS - Exploit Prediction Scoring System. Daily-updated probability (0-1) that a given CVE will be exploited within 30 days. Maintained by FIRST.org.

  • FIDO2 / WebAuthn - Open standards for phishing-resistant authentication. Cryptographically binds the authentication ceremony to the origin domain.

  • IAM - Identity and Access Management. Umbrella term for the systems and processes that authenticate users, manage their credentials, and control access to resources.

  • IdP - Identity Provider. Entra ID, Okta, Ping/ForgeRock (merged 2023), and similar.

  • JIT - Just-in-Time. Privilege model where elevation is granted only on request, with time bounds.

  • LOLBIN - Living-off-the-Land Binary. Legitimate signed system binary repurposed by adversaries to evade allow-listing.

  • MCP - Model Context Protocol. Open standard introduced by Anthropic in late 2024 for AI tools to connect to external data sources and services. Now widely adopted across AI coding assistants. Compromised or malicious MCP servers were a notable attack vector in 2025-26 (see §2.8).

  • MFA - Multi-Factor Authentication. Authentication requiring more than one factor (something you know, have, are). Phishing-resistant variants (FIDO2/WebAuthn, platform passkeys) are the only forms still considered secure against modern AiTM attacks.

  • Mythos Preview - Frontier general-purpose AI model announced by Anthropic in April 2026 with notable cyber capabilities; access restricted via Project Glasswing. Cited as the proximate driver of this article.

  • NRD - Newly Registered Domain. Domain whose registration is recent (commonly under 30 days). High-signal indicator for malicious infrastructure - legitimate enterprise traffic rarely needs to reach a domain registered last week.

  • PAM - Privileged Access Management. Platform category for managing administrative credentials and sessions.

  • RTO - Recovery Time Objective. The target maximum elapsed time between an outage starting and service being restored. The figure documented in BCP/DR plans; in practice the actual time tends to be substantially longer.

  • SBOM - Software Bill of Materials. Machine-readable list of components within a software product. Standard formats: CycloneDX, SPDX.

  • Sigma - Vendor-neutral detection rule format, translatable to most SIEM query languages.

  • SIEM - Security Information and Event Management. Platform that aggregates security telemetry from across the estate, runs detection rules against it, and presents alerts to the SOC.

  • SOAR - Security Orchestration, Automation and Response. Platform category for codifying response playbooks.

  • SPIFFE / SPIRE - Secure Production Identity Framework for Everyone (SPIFFE) and its reference implementation (SPIRE). Open standards for issuing short-lived cryptographic identities (SVIDs) to workloads, used as the basis for service-to-service mTLS without long-lived shared secrets.

  • SSVC - Stakeholder-Specific Vulnerability Categorization. CISA decision-tree framework for prioritisation incorporating mission impact and exposure.

  • TLPT - Threat-Led Penetration Testing. Mandatory under DORA Articles 26-27 for designated significant institutions; equivalent regimes in non-EU jurisdictions (CBEST in UK, etc.).

  • Tier-0/1/2 - Microsoft administrative tier model. Tier-0 = identity and core security infrastructure; Tier-1 = production application servers; Tier-2 = workstation administration. Access between tiers is constrained.

  • WAF - Web Application Firewall. Application-layer filtering, capable of virtual patching for HTTP-exposed vulnerabilities.
