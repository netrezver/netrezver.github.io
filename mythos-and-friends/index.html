Mythos and Friends: Defending Against Frontier-AI-Enabled Cyber Threats

A practitioner's playbook of scenarios and protection measures for financial institutions

1. About this article

Anthropic's announcement of Claude Mythos Preview in April 2026, and the Project Glasswing programme that accompanied it, made one thing concrete that the security community had been discussing in abstract for years: frontier AI models can now find and weaponise software vulnerabilities at machine speed, with minimal human guidance. Independent evaluation by the UK AI Security Institute and others has corroborated the headline claims. Whether or not Mythos itself ever escapes its controlled-access regime, equivalent capabilities are arriving in other models, including ones with far weaker safeguards.

Banks and other financial institutions are exposed in particular ways. They run consequential, real-time, interconnected systems, sit on regulated data and on the means of payment, and face well-funded and unusually patient adversaries. Most importantly, the historical defensive playbook — periodic scans, signature-based detection, perimeter trust, eventually-consistent patch SLAs — was built around threat actors operating at human pace. Speed has changed; the rest of the playbook has not yet caught up.

This article is my attempt at writing down what to actually do about it, in concrete terms. It is meant for cybersecurity and IT engineers: CISOs and their deputies, SOC leads, vulnerability management, IAM, security architecture, detection engineers, incident response, network and endpoint teams. It is not a strategy paper, a regulatory primer, or a vendor comparison. Where it makes sense to be opinionated, I have been — and the underlying view, which I will return to at the end, is that the only realistic answer to AI-augmented attackers is to master the use of AI on the defensive side at least as competently.

How the playbook is structured

Each measure is presented as an action card with a fixed structure: ID (short reference), Title and complexity rating with indicative effort, Risk covered, Attack vector (MITRE ATT&CK tactics), Prerequisites, and Description with optional technical detail.

COMPLEXITY SCALE

Complexity reflects engineering and operational difficulty, not strategic importance:

  • Trivial — configuration change, hours of work, no architectural impact.
  • Low — tooling-supported, days of work, single team.
  • Medium — sprint-scale, weeks, two or three teams in coordination.
  • High — programme-scale, months, multi-team, change management non-trivial.
  • Very High — architectural, multi-quarter, requires new capability or significant reskilling.

Most measures are tunings, accelerations, or extensions of work that mature cyber organisations already do. None should be read as a recommendation to procure a new platform unless the technical detail block explicitly says so. The premise throughout is that the capability you need is usually already deployed somewhere in the estate, and the gap is most often in coverage, tuning, integration, or speed.

2. Operating assumptions and threat model

The measures in this article are written for an adversary that has access — directly, through online services that fail to refuse offensive requests, or through open-weights models — to capabilities of the same class as Anthropic's Claude Mythos Preview. These capabilities are not theoretical. They have been independently corroborated by the UK AI Security Institute and academic groups. The five points below are what I take from that, and what the rest of the article is built on.

2.1 Compressed disclose-to-exploit window

Weaponised exploits for newly disclosed CVEs in widely deployed software now appear within hours of disclosure, not days. This is consistent with the latest ENISA threat-landscape reporting and with observed industry behaviour. Patch SLAs that assume a several-day exploitation lag are obsolete for internet-facing assets, and probably for many internal ones too.

2.2 Autonomous, multi-step adversary operations

An adversary can now run continuous, agentic offensive operations against a target environment for extended periods at low cost, parallelising across many targets. AISI's evaluation showed Mythos completing the full 32-step "The Last Ones" simulated network attack end-to-end. Detection logic that triggers on individual TTPs in isolation will be evaded. What you actually need is detection that fires on the kill chain as a whole.

2.3 Industrialised social engineering

Any individual employee with a public footprint can be the target of bespoke, multi-channel, multi-language social engineering — including voice and video deepfakes referencing real context — at a marginal cost per target that is now near zero. Phishing-resilience programmes built to defend against generic-quality lures are no longer fit for purpose.

2.4 Vendor and open-source dependency exposure

An adversary with frontier-model assistance can find vulnerabilities in almost any non-trivial codebase faster than the code's maintainer can patch them. For most institutions, this exposure is dominated by third-party software, where direct technical mitigation is limited and the only realistic response is structural — blast-radius reduction, SBOM-driven detection, vendor assurance.

2.5 Detection telemetry as adversary target

A sophisticated adversary will try to disable, blind, or evade telemetry collection early in an operation. EDR tampering, log forwarder kill, and timestomp are now baseline expected behaviours. Detection logic must include detection of detection-disablement, otherwise the whole investment becomes worthless the moment the agent goes silent.

2.6 What is not assumed

  • That the adversary has direct access to Mythos Preview itself. The threat is from capability proliferation, not from Mythos as a specific weapon.
  • That no human is in the loop on the offensive side. Frontier models accelerate human attackers, they do not yet replace them.
  • That existing controls are worthless. The point of the article is to identify where existing controls need recalibration, extension, or coverage closure.

2.7 The asymmetry, and what it implies for ambition

The attacker only needs one serious vulnerability to do the job. The defender has to be right about every one of them. That asymmetry was already uncomfortable when humans were doing the looking; it becomes far worse when AI can check them all, automatically, in parallel, around the clock. My belief is that internal cyber metrics should target 100% compliance as their stated ambition — not because anyone realistically expects to hit it, but because anything less builds in tolerance for exactly the kind of single overlooked weakness that a frontier-model-assisted attacker will find first.

2.8 Developer tooling has become its own supply-chain surface

AI coding assistants (GitHub Copilot, Cursor, Claude Code, JetBrains AI, and equivalents) routinely send code context to external APIs as part of normal operation. MCP servers, IDE plugins, and VS Code or JetBrains extensions execute with full IDE process permissions and have direct access to source code, environment variables, and any credentials present on the developer's machine. Several material incidents in 2025 and 2026 have come from poisoned or malicious MCP servers and from compromised marketplace extensions with seven-figure install counts.

As a starting set of minimum controls:

  • An approved-tools register for AI coding assistants and MCP servers. Anything not on the register is denied at the egress proxy and at the IDE plugin manifest level.
  • Plugin and extension governance for VS Code, JetBrains, and equivalents. Block the marketplace's general install path; install from a curated internal mirror only.
  • Network isolation for build agents and CI runners. Build agents should not have outbound access to public AI inference endpoints unless a specific pipeline step requires it.
  • Secret scanning triggered on AI tool activity.
  • Hardware-bound credentials on developer workstations for anything that touches production.

3. Cross-cutting principles

Seven principles run through the measures that follow. They are stated here once so the action cards do not need to repeat them.

3.1
Speed-to-decision over information completeness

Detection and response pipelines have to be set up for fast, defensible decisions on incomplete information. A 30-minute manual triage that ends in certainty is worse than a 2-minute decision that errs on the side of containment, given the pace the adversary is now able to operate at.

3.2
Containment by default

Architecture and IAM choices have to start from the assumption that any single asset, account, or service is compromised. The cost of lateral movement that you can impose on the adversary is the single most useful thing you can invest in.

3.3
Telemetry first

Controls without telemetry are theatrical. Every measure proposed below produces or assumes telemetry. Detection of control failure is itself a control. Saying "we have an EDR" is meaningless without coverage metrics, tamper detection, and a response that has actually been exercised.

3.4
Detect on intent, not artefact

AI-augmented adversaries can mass-produce previously-unseen artefacts (binaries, payloads, infrastructure). Detection logic built on hashes, lists, and signatures will degrade quickly. Detection that fires on observable intent degrades far more slowly.

3.5
Identity is the perimeter

In a hybrid-cloud, SaaS-heavy estate, the network is no longer a meaningful trust boundary. Identity, device posture, and session context are. Controls that act on identity scale across the estate; controls that depend on network position do not.

3.6
Build for the next CVE, not the last incident

Every measure here should be judged on whether it would have helped against the next, unknown vulnerability or campaign, not on whether it would have caught the most recent one.

3.7
The quality of the underlying reference data is non-negotiable

No cyber protection process can be efficient if the reference data underneath it is wrong, stale, or partial. Asset inventories, configuration baselines, ownership registers, network maps, identity catalogues — the ground truth that every other control depends on. Spending on shiny new platforms while the basic registers are unreliable is one of the most expensive mistakes you can make.

4. Short term (0–6 months)

Twelve measures, ordered roughly by sequencing dependency. Most are tuning and coverage-closure work on existing capabilities, plus a small number that require lightweight tooling additions. None require new architecture or major procurement.

S-01
Recalibrate vulnerability SLAs against EPSS, KEV, and exposure
Low 2–4 weeks
Risk
Compromise via newly-disclosed CVE before existing SLA window expires.
ATT&CK
TA0001 Initial Access via T1190 Exploit Public-Facing Application; T1212 Credential Access; T1068 Privilege Escalation.
Prerequisites
Vulnerability scanner (Tenable/Qualys/Rapid7). Asset inventory with internet-exposure flag. CMDB for asset criticality. EPSS and KEV feeds (free).

Replace flat CVSS-driven SLAs with a multi-signal prioritisation model. CVSS measures severity if exploited; EPSS estimates exploitation probability in the next 30 days; KEV confirms current in-the-wild exploitation. Combined with asset exposure and criticality, this allows defensible patch ordering under volume pressure.

Technical detail

Suggested tier definitions for internet-exposed assets:

  • P0 — On KEV, OR EPSS ≥ 0.6, OR vendor advisory references active exploitation. SLA: emergency change, patch within 8h, compensating control within 4h.
  • P1 — CVSS ≥ 8.0 AND EPSS ≥ 0.1, not yet KEV. SLA: 24 hours.
  • P2 — CVSS ≥ 7.0 OR EPSS ≥ 0.05. SLA: 7 days.
  • P3 — Below thresholds. SLA: standard 30-day cycle.

For non-internet-exposed assets, shift each tier by one level. EPSS feed: https://epss.empiricalsecurity.com/epss_scores-current.csv.gz

# Daily EPSS ingestion (cron @ 06:00 UTC)
curl -s https://epss.empiricalsecurity.com/epss_scores-current.csv.gz | \
  gunzip | \
  awk -F, 'NR>2 && $2>=0.05 {print $1","$2","$3}' > /var/data/epss-high.csv
# Join with scanner output keyed on CVE-ID
# Persist daily snapshots — EPSS trend matters more than absolute value
S-02
Establish an emergency-change pathway for security-driven changes
Low 2–3 weeks
Risk
Existing change-management cycle becomes the bottleneck on patch speed.
ATT&CK
Same as S-01.
Prerequisites
Existing change-management process. Engineering rota. Pre-defined reviewer set with delegated authority.

Create a pre-authorised, well-instrumented emergency change pathway specifically for security patches and compensating controls, invokable by the SOC duty officer or vulnerability-management lead without case-by-case CAB approval, with mandatory after-the-fact review.

Technical detail — minimum control set
  • Two-person authorisation in the change-management tool, one from a defined cyber role.
  • Automatic notification to system owner and CISO duty officer.
  • Mandatory rollback test or pre-staged rollback plan.
  • Twenty-four-hour after-action review.
  • Quarterly metrics review: rate of use, false-emergency rate, post-incident review of all uses.
S-03
Internet-facing attack surface inventory and reduction
Low 4–6 weeks
Risk
Unknown or undocumented internet-exposed assets become path of least resistance for autonomous reconnaissance.
ATT&CK
TA0043 Reconnaissance T1595, T1592; TA0001 Initial Access T1190.
Prerequisites
External ASM capability (Censys, Shodan-based ASM, Defender EASM, Mandiant ASM). Authoritative DNS records.

Run a fresh external attack-surface discovery against all known IP ranges, registered domains, and cloud-tenant boundaries. Reconcile against the asset inventory. Triage every unmatched finding: (1) legitimate but unrecorded, (2) abandoned and removable, (3) shadow IT.

Technical detail — high-value findings
  • Authentication portals (RDP, SSH, VPN admin, Citrix, vCenter) reachable from the public internet without IP allow-listing.
  • Cloud storage buckets with public read/write.
  • Subdomain takeover candidates (CNAME pointing at decommissioned cloud resources).
  • Exposed management planes for SaaS connectors (CI/CD runners, observability platforms).
  • Forgotten WAF-bypass routes (hostnames resolving directly to origin IPs).

Treat every unmatched finding as P1 by default. Force a within-72h decision: remove vs. record vs. accept.

S-04
Phishing-simulation programme uplift for AI-grade lures
Low 6–8 weeks
Risk
Personnel compromise via high-fidelity, multi-channel social engineering (text + voice + video).
ATT&CK
TA0001 T1566 Phishing (.001, .002, .003); T1684.001 Impersonation; T1598 Phishing for Information.
Prerequisites
Existing phishing-simulation platform (KnowBe4, Cofense, Hoxhunt). HR list of high-value populations.

Refresh the simulation programme to include language-localised, contextually personalised lures generated with current-generation tools. Move from annual to quarterly cadence for high-value populations; include voice (synthesised audio) and where feasible video deepfake exposures.

Technical detail
  • Multi-language lures matching the languages of the target population (not English only).
  • Personalisation drawing on legitimate public context (recent announcements, LinkedIn activity, conference attendance).
  • Voice-call follow-up referencing a real meeting, for the executive simulation cohort.
  • A small number of "impossible" deepfake video lures per cycle for the executive cohort, with mandatory debrief.

Out-of-band verification protocol: any request for payment authorisation, credential disclosure, urgent access provisioning, or signing of unfamiliar documents must be verified through a pre-agreed second channel.

S-05
Privileged-access tightening: just-in-time elevation and session recording
Medium 3–4 months
Risk
Standing administrative privilege gives the adversary a small set of very high-value credentials to steal.
ATT&CK
TA0004 T1078 Valid Accounts; TA0008 T1021 Remote Services; TA0006 Credential Access.
Prerequisites
Existing PAM platform (CyberArk, Delinea, BeyondTrust, HashiCorp Vault). Authoritative privileged account inventory. Tier-0/1/2 model defined.

Reduce standing administrative privilege to the minimum compatible with operations. Implement just-in-time elevation with explicit time-bounded grants for all interactive admin sessions. Enable session recording for Tier-0 and Tier-1 activity, with recordings written to write-once storage outside the administered domain.

Technical detail — tier model
  • Tier-0 — directory services, IdPs, CAs, KMS. Standing privilege: zero. Approval: named human in another team. Max session: 4 hours, recorded.
  • Tier-1 — production app admins, DBAs, hypervisor admins. Standing privilege: zero. Approval: queue-based on-call gate. Max session: 8 hours.
  • Tier-2 — workstation admin, helpdesk. Standing privilege: limited per-task. JIT for everything else.
S-06
MFA hardening: phishing-resistant factors for high-value workflows
Medium 3–6 months
Risk
Push-bombing, MFA fatigue, and AiTM proxy attacks defeat first-generation MFA.
ATT&CK
T1621 MFA Request Generation; T1556.006 Modify Authentication; T1111 MFA Interception.
Prerequisites
Existing MFA deployment. IdP supporting WebAuthn/FIDO2 (Entra ID, Okta, Ping/ForgeRock). Hardware key procurement budget for high-value populations.

Migrate from push-notification or TOTP MFA to phishing-resistant factors (WebAuthn/FIDO2, platform passkeys, or hardware tokens such as YubiKey 5) for all high-value populations and all administrative authentication. Phishing-resistant MFA defeats AiTM proxy attacks because the WebAuthn challenge is cryptographically bound to the origin domain.

Technical detail — migration sequence
  • Phase 1: enable WebAuthn alongside existing factors. No removal yet.
  • Phase 2: enforce WebAuthn-only for administrative and Tier-0/Tier-1 access.
  • Phase 3: enforce for high-value workflows (payment authorisation, treasury, M&A).
  • Phase 4: extend to general user population, with break-glass alternative for hardware loss.
policy: "admin-mfa-fido2-only"
  conditions:
    users: group("PrivilegedRoleAssignees")
    applications: any
  controls:
    grant:
      authentication_strength: "phishing_resistant_mfa"
      block_legacy_auth: true
S-07
EDR coverage and tamper-detection audit
Low 3–4 weeks
Risk
Endpoint compromise on assets where EDR is absent, mis-deployed, or silenced by the adversary.
ATT&CK
TA0005 T1562.001 Disable/Modify Tools; T1070 Indicator Removal; T1218 System Binary Proxy Execution.
Prerequisites
Existing EDR platform (CrowdStrike, SentinelOne, Defender for Endpoint). Authoritative endpoint inventory.

Reconcile the EDR deployment census against the endpoint inventory. Implement tamper-detection alerting that fires when an EDR agent stops reporting, is uninstalled, or has its kernel components modified — at the same priority as a confirmed malicious-binary detection.

Technical detail — signals to alert on
  • Agent service stopped, paused, or uninstalled — telemetry interruption longer than a tunable threshold (5–15 min for endpoints, 1 min for servers).
  • Kernel driver or system extension unloading on the protected endpoint.
  • Local administrator group modifications outside change windows.
  • Group Policy or MDM policy changes affecting EDR posture.
  • Suspicious use of vendor-supplied uninstaller tools.

Alerts on tamper events must NOT be suppressible by the local administrator or by the host-agent itself.

S-08
Identity-protection alerts: impossible travel, token theft, and session anomalies
Low 2–4 weeks
Risk
Token theft bypasses MFA entirely once the session is established.
ATT&CK
T1539 Steal Web Session Cookie; T1528 Steal Application Access Token; T1550.004.
Prerequisites
IdP with risk-based authentication (Entra ID Identity Protection, Okta ThreatInsight, PingOne Protect). SIEM with IdP log ingestion. Geo-IP enrichment.

Most of these signals are already available in the IdP — most banks I've seen have them licensed and unconfigured. Token theft is one of the most under-detected real-world attack patterns in 2026. WebAuthn (S-06) hardens the front door, but it does not stop a stolen session token being replayed from attacker infrastructure.

Technical detail — detection logic
  • Impossible travel — same user, two authentications from geographically inconsistent locations.
  • Anonymous IP — sign-in from Tor, known VPN exit, or hosting-provider IP.
  • Unusual sign-in properties — new device, browser, ASN combination on a high-privilege account.
  • Token issued from one IP, used from another (especially: issued from corporate VPN, used from cloud-hosting IP within minutes).
  • Service-principal anomalies — OAuth consent grants to newly-registered apps; high-privilege scope grants.
S-09
Egress filtering and DNS sinkholing for known-bad infrastructure
Low 4–6 weeks
Risk
C2 beaconing, exfiltration, and second-stage payload retrieval over outbound connections from compromised internal hosts.
ATT&CK
TA0011 C2 T1071, T1568; T1041 Exfiltration Over C2; T1567 Exfiltration Over Web Service.
Prerequisites
Egress proxy or NGFW with TLS-inspection or SNI-based filtering. DNS infrastructure capable of ingesting block-lists. Threat-intelligence feed (Spamhaus, abuse.ch).

Server populations typically have very narrow legitimate outbound destinations. Any deviation is signal. Block-listing of known-malicious infrastructure at DNS and egress proxy, combined with anomaly detection on outbound traffic from server populations that should not be making arbitrary outbound calls.

Technical detail — three holes SNI/DNS filtering does not close
  • DNS-over-HTTPS (DoH) — Force all DNS to internal resolvers; block known DoH provider IP ranges; deny outbound TCP/443 and UDP/443 to those endpoints from workstation segments.
  • QUIC (UDP/443) — SNI inspection is TCP-layer; QUIC slips past it. Deny outbound UDP/443 from workstations except where explicitly allow-listed.
  • Direct-to-IP HTTPS — Traffic that never resolves through the corporate resolver. Deny direct-to-IP outbound :443 from workstations.
S-10
Critical-asset patch tabletop and dry-run
Low 4 weeks
Risk
Time spent rediscovering basic facts during a P0 response dominates the actual response time.
Prerequisites
S-01 and S-02 in place. CMDB with application-to-infrastructure mapping. List of top internet-exposed application stacks.

Run a structured tabletop covering a realistic P0 scenario for the top internet-exposed application stacks with actual on-call personnel. Time the responses. Identify and document the actual blockers — in my experience these are usually not technical but procedural (who can authorise a customer-impacting change at 03:00, who notifies the regulator).

S-11
Service-account inventory and rotation programme launch
Medium 3–6 months
Risk
Long-lived, broadly-permissioned, forgotten service accounts are a primary lateral-movement target.
ATT&CK
T1078.002 Domain Accounts; T1078.004 Cloud Accounts; T1098 Account Manipulation.
Prerequisites
Directory services (AD, Entra ID, Okta) with audit logging. CMDB with service ownership. Vault platform (HashiCorp Vault, CyberArk Conjur, cloud-native KMS).

Build and maintain an authoritative inventory of all non-human identities. For each: owner, purpose, scope of access, credential type, last rotation, last use, attached compensating controls. Eliminate the long tail (unused, unowned, over-permissioned). Establish rotation cadence by tier.

Technical detail — inventory data model

Account name, type, owning team, technical contact, business application, permissions held, credential metadata (last rotation, expiry, vault location), recent activity (last successful auth, source IP distribution).

Eliminate first, rotate second. Target removal: any account with no successful authentication in 90 days without a documented exception.

S-12
AI-augmented adversary scenarios in current TLPT cycle
Medium Current cycle
Risk
TLPT exercises designed against pre-2026 adversary capability now systematically under-test the actual threat.
Prerequisites
Active TLPT cycle (TIBER-EU, CBEST, or local equivalent). Threat Intelligence Provider engaged.

In coordination with the appointed Threat Intelligence Provider, refresh the Targeted Threat Intelligence Report to explicitly include AI-augmented adversary TTPs: rapid vulnerability discovery in public-facing custom code, multi-language phishing at scale, voice/deepfake-augmented social engineering, autonomous lateral movement via chained low-severity findings.

5. Medium term (6–18 months)

Eleven measures. Programme-scale efforts that industrialise and extend the short-term work. Most need coordination across several engineering teams, dedicated programme management, and incremental tooling investment. They build directly on the short-term measures.

M-01
Continuous attack-surface management with automated revalidation
Medium6–9 months
Depends on
S-03

Move external attack-surface management from a one-time discovery exercise to a daily-cadence continuous capability with auto-ticketing of unmatched findings. Integrate with cloud-tenant inventory so that newly-provisioned cloud resources appear in the EASM view within hours. Pair with internal ASM including identity attack-path analysis (BloodHound Enterprise).

M-02
SBOM-driven vulnerability response for first-party software
High9–15 months

Produce a Software Bill of Materials for every internally-developed application at build time, in CycloneDX or SPDX format. Store SBOMs centrally with build provenance. On CVE disclosure, query the SBOM repository to identify all affected applications within minutes.

# In CI, after build:
syft packages dir:./build -o cyclonedx-json > sbom.cdx.json

# Push to central SBOM store:
curl -X POST -H "Authorization: Bearer $TOKEN" \
  --data-binary @sbom.cdx.json \
  https://sbom-store.internal/v1/sbom/$APP/$VERSION

# On CVE alert:
curl https://sbom-store.internal/v1/affected?cve=CVE-2026-XXXXX
# → list of all (app, version) pairs containing the vulnerable component
M-03
Microsegmentation of Tier-0/Tier-1 systems
High12–18 months
ATT&CK
TA0008 Lateral Movement: T1021, T1570, T1210.
Prerequisites
Microsegmentation platform (Illumio, Akamai Guardicore, Cisco Secure Workload). Application dependency mapping.

Implement default-deny lateral connectivity for Tier-0 and Tier-1 systems. Allow only explicitly defined east-west flows. Of all measures in this article, this is by far the most demanding to deliver. The technology has been mature for years — the difficulty is operational.

Technical detail — sequencing
  • Phase 1 — discovery only. Deploy in observe mode 4–8 weeks per workload group.
  • Phase 2 — author policies in dry-run. Compare predicted blocks against observed traffic.
  • Phase 3 — enforcement on a single low-stakes Tier-1 application as canary. 30 days monitoring.
  • Phase 4 — staged rollout by criticality. Special-case Tier-0 first as an early win.
M-04
Service-account credential rotation industrialisation
High12–18 months
Depends on
S-11

Migrate service-account credential consumption from "vault dispenses, application caches forever" to short-lived, dynamically-issued credentials. For cloud-native: cloud-IAM workload identity (IRSA on AWS, workload identity on GCP, managed identities on Azure). For on-premise: Vault dynamic secrets or X.509 certificate-based authentication with short rotation.

M-05
Detection-engineering programme: behavioural rules and ATT&CK coverage
Medium9–12 months initial

Establish or scale a detection-engineering practice that produces, tests, deploys, and retires detections on a development-team cadence. Organise rule coverage against MITRE ATT&CK to expose blind spots. Rules are versioned in Git, tested against generated adversary behaviour, and have explicit signal/noise contracts.

Technical detail — priority behavioural detections
  • Authentication primitive abuse — Kerberoasting (T1558.003), AS-REP roasting, Golden/Silver Ticket, DCSync.
  • Process injection lineage — code creating threads in remote processes (T1055), LSASS access patterns.
  • Living-off-the-land — abnormal sequences of LOLBINs (rundll32, regsvr32, mshta, certutil, bitsadmin) with network egress.
  • Cloud-control-plane recon — bulk enumerate-style API calls (List*, Describe*) from new principals.
  • Beaconing — periodic outbound connections with low jitter from server populations.
M-06
Deception infrastructure (canary tokens and decoy assets)
Medium6–9 months
ATT&CK
TA0007 Discovery — every technique is a deception trigger by design.

Canary tokens — fake credentials, files, AWS keys, mailboxes embedded in legitimate-looking locations. Any access to a canary is, by construction, unauthorised activity. Decoy assets — full or partial fake hosts and services. Deception is one of the few defensive investments where the false-positive rate is structurally near zero.

Technical detail — high-signal placements
  • Fake AWS keys in developer workstation .aws/credentials files.
  • Honeydocs named "Network Diagram - Q4 2026.docx" or "Privileged Account Vault Backup.xlsx" on file shares.
  • DNS records for plausible-but-fake internal hosts (internal-vault, scm-backup, payroll-archive).
  • Synthetic credentials committed to repos and "removed" in a later commit but recoverable via git log -p. Particularly worth deploying in M&A diligence repositories.
M-07
AI-assisted SOC triage and analyst augmentation
Medium9–12 months
Depends on
M-05

Deploy frontier-model assistance to specific SOC workflows: alert summarisation, evidence aggregation, suggested next-pivot, and draft IR write-up. Do not delegate response decisions to the model. The pattern is augmentation of human analysts, with model output framed as suggestion and the human as decision-maker.

Technical detail — prompt injection risk

The pipelines feeding the SOC LLM — log lines, alert payloads, file names, process command lines, DNS labels — contain attacker-controlled fields. An adversary can embed prompt-injection payloads in any of these to instruct the model to suppress an alert or recommend the wrong pivot. Mitigations:

  • Treat any model input derived from attacker-controlled fields as untrusted. Quarantine and quote-escape such fields before they reach the prompt.
  • Enforce structured output schemas (JSON with named fields).
  • The analyst reviews the raw alert, not just the model summary, before any containment decision.
  • Log every model input and output for retrospective audit.
M-08
Pre-staged compensating controls for unpatchable conditions
Medium6–9 months
Depends on
S-01

For each system marked patch-infeasible-in-SLA, document a pre-staged compensating control that can be applied at P0/P1 without further design: virtual patch rule on the WAF, ACL change at the segment boundary, complete network isolation with documented business-impact, or application-layer mitigation by configuration change. Held by the SOC, reviewed quarterly, dry-run annually.

M-09
Third-party software: SBOM-and-patch-cadence procurement requirements
Medium12–24 months

Add to procurement standard terms at next renewal: (1) vendor produces and shares SBOMs on each release; (2) vendor commits to maximum patch cadence for critical vulnerabilities (target: 7 days for KEV-listed); (3) vendor notifies of any active exploitation within a defined window; (4) vendor permits compensating controls (does not contractually forbid WAF rules). This is procurement and legal work, not engineering work.

M-10
Recovery rehearsal: ransomware-grade incident drill against Tier-1 application
HighFirst run 6–9 months
Depends on
M-11

Run an end-to-end recovery drill against one Tier-1 application, treating it as if compromised by ransomware-class destructive attack. The drill must be unannounced to the application owner team beyond the date window. Run with actual on-call personnel. Time every step. The output is a remediation list of every assumption that proved incorrect. In my experience, actual recovery times tend to run several multiples longer than the figure documented in the BCP.

M-11
Backup immutability and recovery-path validation
High9–12 months
ATT&CK
T1490 Inhibit System Recovery; T1485 Data Destruction; T1486 Data Encrypted for Impact.

Enforce immutability or true air-gap for the recovery copy of every Tier-0 and Tier-1 system. The objective is that no operationally-resident credential can delete or encrypt the recovery tier within its retention window. Validate this independently rather than relying on the platform's marketing.

Technical detail — minimum control set
  • Immutability at storage layer (S3 Object Lock compliance mode, Azure immutable blobs). Retention ≥ 90 days.
  • Backup admin credentials in a separate identity domain from production admin credentials.
  • Air-gapped tier (cold offline copy) for the most critical 5–10 systems, restored from independently at least annually.
  • Automated test restore on a defined cadence (weekly for top tier, monthly for the rest), with the restored system actually booted and smoke-tested.

6. Long term (18+ months)

Six measures, all architectural in nature. These are changes that take quarters to design and years to land. Listing something here is not a commitment to do it — it is a flag that the work needs evaluation and, if pursued, programme-grade governance.

L-01
Identity-centric zero-trust architecture across the application estate
Very High24–36 months
Depends on
S-06, S-08, M-03, M-04

Replace the existing perimeter-and-VPN model with a zero-trust architecture where every application access is authenticated, authorised, and continuously evaluated against device posture and session risk, regardless of network origin. East-west traffic encrypted and authenticated via service mesh. Application access brokered through identity-aware proxies.

L-02
Microsegmentation extended to the full estate
Very High24–36 months
Depends on
M-03

Extend the microsegmentation programme from Tier-0/Tier-1 systems to the rest of the production estate. Scoped to the long term because the operational learning curve from M-03 takes 12–18 months to consolidate before the team is ready to scale the pattern to dozens of additional application groups.

L-03
Detection coverage maturity to assessed parity with adversary kill chains
Very High24–36 months continuous
Depends on
M-05

Maturity destination for the detection programme: every ATT&CK technique relevant to the institution's environment has at least one detection of documented signal-to-noise quality. Purple-team exercises run continuously. A note on what is realistically achievable: even mature programmes do not reach 100% ATT&CK coverage. In my experience the curve tends to flatten somewhere in the 60–70% range.

L-04
AI-on-AI defence: autonomous response with bounded action authority
Very High24+ months
Depends on
M-05, M-07

Define a small, well-bounded set of containment actions that can be taken autonomously on high-confidence detections without human approval. Examples: isolating a non-production endpoint, disabling a non-privileged user session, blocking a specific outbound destination at the proxy. The boundary is what is reversible and low-blast-radius.

Technical detail — action-authority boundaries
  • Permitted autonomous: isolating a workstation in a non-production segment on confirmed C2; revoking a session token on confirmed token-theft; blocking a specific outbound IP on confirmed C2.
  • Always human-approved: any action affecting customer-facing services; any account disable for a privileged user; any production-server isolation; any cloud-tenant-wide control change.
  • Forbidden: autonomous offensive action. Defensive only.

The system must include an emergency stop that disables all autonomy on operator command.

L-05
Targeted replacement of unsupportable legacy infrastructure
Very HighAligned to IT modernisation
Depends on
M-08

Identify the specific legacy systems that are the locus of the highest residual risk after compensating controls have been applied, and integrate their replacement into the IT modernisation roadmap with cyber-posture as an explicit prioritisation criterion. This is a security input into the existing modernisation prioritisation process, not a security-led programme.

L-06
Sectoral defensive co-operation participation
MediumOngoing

Maintain and extend participation in sectoral information-sharing channels with explicit focus on AI-augmented adversary intelligence: TTP observations, indicator sharing, vendor vulnerability coordination. Listed in the long-term section because the structural co-operation channels beyond what FS-ISAC already provides are not yet established. The action today is to participate in their formation.

8. Two things I keep coming back to

8.1 The best defence against an AI attacker is mastering AI defence. The pace, scale, and parallelism that frontier AI gives to attackers cannot be matched by human defenders alone. We should not pretend otherwise. The institutions that come out of this period in the strongest position will be the ones that learn to apply frontier AI on the defensive side at least as competently as their adversaries are applying it on the offensive side — in detection, in triage, in vulnerability discovery, in red teaming, eventually in bounded autonomous response (L-04). That is not an abdication of human judgement. It is an honest read of where the asymmetry is, and the only realistic way I see of closing it.

8.2 The knowledge is already in the technical teams; management has to make it safe to surface. In every organisation I have worked with, there is a striking amount of awareness within the engineering, SOC, and infrastructure teams about exactly where the weak points are. People know which systems are out of patch cycle, which controls are bypassed in practice, which alerts get ignored, which compensating controls only exist on paper. What changes from one organisation to another is whether that knowledge surfaces upwards proactively, or whether it stays buried until an incident drags it out under unflattering circumstances. This is not a technical problem. It is a management one. I would go further and say that the gap between organisations that handle AI-augmented threats well and ones that do not will be explained more by this management posture than by any single technical investment.

A.3 Beaconing detection (T1071, T1568.002)

# Scheduled query against proxy/firewall logs (every 30 minutes)
events
| where event.kind == 'network' and direction == 'outbound'
| where source.ip in (server_segment_cidrs)
| bin time = bin(@timestamp, 1m)
| summarize
    connections = count(),
    distinct_dst = dcount(destination.domain),
    intervals = make_list(time)
    by source.ip, destination.domain
| extend interval_stddev = series_stddev(intervals_to_diffs(intervals))
| where connections >= 12      # at least 12 events in window
| where interval_stddev < 5   # low jitter
| where distinct_dst <= 2     # narrow set of destinations
| project source.ip, destination.domain, connections, interval_stddev

A.6 AI-specific patterns (ATT&CK v19 T1682, T1683)

Pattern 6.1 — bulk LLM API egress from non-developer populations. Outbound connections to public inference endpoints (api.openai.com, api.anthropic.com, generativelanguage.googleapis.com) from server populations or unauthorised workstation populations. Tune against the institution's approved-AI-tools allow-list.

Pattern 6.2 — prompt-injection signal in attacker-controlled fields. Look for instruction-formatted content in fields that feed AI tooling: long Base64 blobs in User-Agent strings, SYSTEM: or ### Instruction tokens in DNS labels, command lines, or process names, and unusual unicode patterns (zero-width characters, RTL overrides) in fields the SOC LLM reads.

Pattern 6.3 — AI-assisted credential stuffing. High-volume authentication failures distributed across many source IPs (rotated proxies) but with suspiciously homogeneous TLS fingerprints (JA3/JA4) or user-agent strings — the signature of a single tool driving many endpoints.

Annex B — Vulnerability prioritisation reference

B.1 The four signals

  • CVSS — Technical severity if exploited. Range 0–10. Use v3.1 base score until v4 is universally adopted.
  • EPSS — Probability of exploitation in the next 30 days (0–1). Updated daily. v4 model live since March 2025. API: https://api.first.org/data/v1/epss
  • KEV — CISA Known Exploited Vulnerabilities. Confirmed in-the-wild exploitation. Treat as P0 regardless of CVSS or EPSS. Feed: https://www.cisa.gov/kev
  • Asset context — Internet-exposed? Tier-0/Tier-1? Holds regulated data? Patchable in SLA?

B.2 Decision matrix

TierTrigger condition (any)SLA (internet-exposed)
P0 — emergencyOn KEV; OR EPSS ≥ 0.6; OR vendor advisory references active exploitationPatch within 8h; compensating control within 4h
P1 — pre-emergencyCVSS ≥ 8.0 AND EPSS ≥ 0.1, not yet KEVPatch within 24h
P2 — highCVSS ≥ 7.0 OR EPSS ≥ 0.05Patch within 7 days
P3 — standardBelow P2 thresholdsStandard 30-day cycle

For non-internet-exposed assets, shift each tier by one level. For Tier-0 assets, keep internet-exposed SLAs even if the asset is internal.

B.3 EPSS trend signal

Persist daily EPSS snapshots. A CVE whose EPSS doubles in a week deserves attention even if its current absolute value is moderate. This early-warning signal frequently precedes KEV listing by days. Practical rule: trigger only when the doubling condition AND the new value is at or above 0.02.

B.4 Caveats

  • EPSS can underestimate a vulnerability exploited in targeted operations against specific industries before broad exploitation begins. Maintain a manual override path for threat intelligence on financial-sector-targeted CVEs.
  • KEV is reactive — by the time a CVE is added, broad exploitation is already happening. Use EPSS as the leading signal.
  • Vendor advisories sometimes precede public CVE assignment. Treat "actively exploited" language in a vendor advisory as P0 regardless of whether the CVE is yet on KEV.

Annex C — Glossary

  • AiTM — Adversary-in-the-Middle. Phishing pattern where the attacker proxies the legitimate authentication site, capturing credentials and session tokens. Defeated by phishing-resistant MFA bound to the origin domain.
  • ASM / EASM — Attack Surface Management / External Attack Surface Management. Continuous discovery of internet-exposed assets attributable to the organisation.
  • ATT&CK — MITRE adversary tactics and techniques framework. ATT&CK v19 (released 28 April 2026) introduced changes including splitting the Defense Evasion tactic and reorganising social-engineering techniques under T1684.
  • CISA KEV — US Cybersecurity and Infrastructure Security Agency Known Exploited Vulnerabilities catalogue. Free feed; daily updates.
  • CMDB — Configuration Management Database. The authoritative register of IT assets, services, ownership, and relationships.
  • CVSS — Common Vulnerability Scoring System. Severity score from 0 to 10.
  • EDR — Endpoint Detection and Response. Agent-based endpoint security platform.
  • EPSS — Exploit Prediction Scoring System. Daily-updated probability (0–1) that a given CVE will be exploited within 30 days. Maintained by FIRST.org.
  • FIDO2 / WebAuthn — Open standards for phishing-resistant authentication. Cryptographically binds the authentication ceremony to the origin domain.
  • JIT — Just-in-Time. Privilege model where elevation is granted only on request, with time bounds.
  • LOLBIN — Living-off-the-Land Binary. Legitimate signed system binary repurposed by adversaries to evade allow-listing.
  • MCP — Model Context Protocol. Open standard introduced by Anthropic in late 2024 for AI tools to connect to external data sources and services. Compromised or malicious MCP servers were a notable attack vector in 2025–26.
  • MFA — Multi-Factor Authentication. Phishing-resistant variants (FIDO2/WebAuthn, platform passkeys) are the only forms still considered secure against modern AiTM attacks.
  • Mythos Preview — Frontier general-purpose AI model announced by Anthropic in April 2026 with notable cyber capabilities; access restricted via Project Glasswing. Cited as the proximate driver of this article.
  • NRD — Newly Registered Domain. Domain whose registration is recent (commonly under 30 days). High-signal indicator for malicious infrastructure.
  • PAM — Privileged Access Management. Platform category for managing administrative credentials and sessions.
  • RTO — Recovery Time Objective. The target maximum elapsed time between an outage starting and service being restored.
  • SBOM — Software Bill of Materials. Machine-readable list of components within a software product. Standard formats: CycloneDX, SPDX.
  • Sigma — Vendor-neutral detection rule format, translatable to most SIEM query languages.
  • SIEM — Security Information and Event Management. Platform aggregating security telemetry and running detection rules.
  • SOAR — Security Orchestration, Automation and Response. Platform for codifying response playbooks.
  • SPIFFE / SPIRE — Open standards for issuing short-lived cryptographic identities (SVIDs) to workloads, used as the basis for service-to-service mTLS.
  • SSVC — Stakeholder-Specific Vulnerability Categorization. CISA decision-tree framework for prioritisation incorporating mission impact and exposure.
  • TLPT — Threat-Led Penetration Testing. Mandatory under DORA Articles 26–27 for designated significant institutions; equivalent: CBEST (UK), TIBER-EU.
  • Tier-0/1/2 — Microsoft administrative tier model. Tier-0 = identity and core security infrastructure; Tier-1 = production application servers; Tier-2 = workstation administration.
  • WAF — Web Application Firewall. Application-layer filtering, capable of virtual patching for HTTP-exposed vulnerabilities.
